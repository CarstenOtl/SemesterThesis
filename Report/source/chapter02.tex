\chapter{Related Work}%
\label{ch:related_work}%

This chapter reviews prior work along the three axes most relevant to this thesis: dexterous grasping with multi-fingered hands, privileged policy distillation, and sim-to-real transfer through domain randomization.
Each section traces how the field evolved, highlights the strengths and limitations of each paradigm, and motivates the choices made in this work.

\section{Dexterous Grasping with Multi-Fingered Hands}%
\label{sec:dexterous_grasping}%

Dexterous grasping with anthropomorphic hands has been studied for decades, but only recently have learned policies begun to outperform classical pipelines on unstructured object sets.
This section contrasts analytical and learning-based paradigms, tracing how the limitations of the former motivated the shift to the latter.

\subsection{Analytical and Data-Driven Grasp Planning}%
\label{subsec:rule_based_grasping}%

Classical robotic grasping formulates the problem as finding contact points on a known object surface that satisfy force closure, meaning that the contact wrenches alone can resist arbitrary external loads~\cite{Bicchi2000, Ferrari1992}.
Given a 3D object model and friction parameters, analytical planners search for contact configurations that maximize a quality metric such as the largest wrench ball inscribable in the \ac{GWS}~\cite{Ferrari1992}.
For parallel-jaw grippers in structured industrial settings, these methods have been highly successful.
In practice, classical dexterous systems organize perception, grasp planning, trajectory generation, and post-grasp manipulation as independent pipeline stages with hand-tuned parameters per robot and object set~\cite{Bicchi2000, Siciliano2009}.

Later work replaced the combinatorial search with data-driven methods while retaining the analytical grasp-quality objective.
DexGraspNet~\cite{Wang2023} generated 1.32 million grasps for over 5{,}000 objects using a differentiable force-closure estimator, and Wu et al.~\cite{Wu2022} demonstrated 86.7\,\% grasp success on novel objects with a learned generative model.

The principal advantage of analytical and data-driven planning is interpretability: grasp quality can be verified against well-understood physical criteria before execution.
However, these methods share two fundamental limitations.
First, the search space grows to $\mathrm{SE}(3) \times \mathbb{R}^{n_h}$ for an $n_h$-jointed hand, making exhaustive planning intractable for multi-fingered hands~\cite{Bicchi2000}.
Second, all variants produce static grasp configurations that are executed by a separate controller and cannot adapt if conditions differ from the assumed model; whenever the object geometry, contact physics, or robot kinematics are wrong, as is unavoidable for novel objects, deformable materials, or worn hardware, the planned grasp fails~\cite{Tang2024}.
Errors compound across the pipeline, and the absence of online adaptation prevents recovery from perturbations.

\subsection{Reinforcement Learning}%
\label{subsec:learning_based_grasping}%

The static, non-adaptive nature and model dependence of analytical methods motivate a fundamentally different paradigm: \ac{RL}, which jointly learns perception, grasp strategy, and reactive control from interaction~\cite{Tang2024}.
Instead of relying on pre-specified grasp taxonomies, the policy discovers contact strategies through trial and error in simulation, producing closed-loop controllers that can react to perturbations at every time step.

Kumar et al.~\cite{Kumar2016} were among the first to demonstrate model-based \ac{RL} for contact-rich manipulation on a 24-\acs{DoF} hand.
Andrychowicz et al.~\cite{Andrychowicz2020} then showed that a Shadow Dexterous Hand could learn to solve a Rubik's Cube through massive-scale \ac{RL} in simulation followed by sim-to-real transfer, establishing the viability of learned dexterous manipulation on real hardware.
DeXtreme~\cite{Handa2024} combined vectorized \ac{ADR} with a vision-based pose estimator for robust in-hand cube reorientation on a real Allegro Hand.
DexPBT~\cite{Petrenko2023} applied population-based training to discover reward weights and hyperparameters automatically.
UniDexGrasp++~\cite{Wan2023} decomposed the problem into a learned grasp proposal stage followed by goal-conditioned \ac{RL} execution, achieving generalization to novel objects.

While much of this work targets in-hand manipulation with a fixed wrist, real applications require coordinated arm-hand control: reaching, grasping, and transporting an object as a single behavior.
DextrAH-G~\cite{Lum2024} addressed this with a 23-motor arm-hand system, combining geometric fabrics~\cite{Ratliff2023} as a structured low-level control layer with an \ac{RL} policy on top, achieving fast and safe dexterous grasping from depth images.
DextrAH-RGB~\cite{Singh2025} extended this to RGB input and demonstrated the first successful sim-to-real transfer of an RGB-based dexterous grasping policy.

The key advantage of learned policies is closed-loop reactivity and the ability to handle novel objects without explicit models.
The key limitation is the sim-to-real gap: policies trained with privileged simulation state (object poses, contact forces) do not directly transfer to a real robot that observes only camera images and joint encoders.
This gap motivates the privileged teacher--student distillation paradigm discussed in the next section.
\textbf{This thesis builds on DextrAH-RGB but trains the teacher as a standard \ac{RL} policy without geometric fabrics}, accepting a less constrained controller in exchange for a simpler training stack and the ability to use a custom hand asset (the Agile Hand on a Franka Research~3).

\section{Privileged Policy Distillation}%
\label{sec:distillation}%

The sim-to-real gap identified in the previous section means that the most effective simulation-trained policies, which rely on privileged state such as object poses and contact forces, cannot be deployed directly.
Bridging this gap requires transferring the teacher's behavior to a student that observes only what is available on the real robot.

\emph{Knowledge distillation}, introduced by Hinton et al.~\cite{Hinton2015}, was originally developed to compress a large teacher network into a smaller student network with minimal loss in accuracy.
The core idea is to train the student to reproduce the teacher's output distribution rather than the ground-truth labels alone, so that the student inherits not only the teacher's decisions but also its uncertainty structure.
An alternative is the \emph{Mixture of Experts} (MoE) paradigm, which combines multiple specialist sub-networks through a learned gating mechanism rather than consolidating knowledge into a single student.

Privileged teacher--student distillation in robotics adapts the distillation framework to a different problem: not model compression, but \emph{modality transfer}.
The teacher operates on privileged simulation state (object poses, contact forces), while the student must produce the same behavior from deployable observations such as RGB images and proprioception~\cite{Lum2024, Singh2025}.
Because teacher and student observe fundamentally different inputs, the student cannot simply copy the teacher's internal representations; instead, it must learn a new perception front-end while imitating the teacher's actions.
This makes the distillation step an imitation-learning problem, and the quality of the transfer depends critically on how the training data is collected.
The following subsections discuss the imitation-learning algorithms used for this purpose.

\subsection{Imitation Learning and DAgger}%
\label{subsec:bc_dagger}%

\ac{BC} fits a policy by supervised regression on a fixed dataset of teacher state--action pairs~\cite{Pomerleau1988}.
It is straightforward but brittle: small deviations from the teacher's state distribution lead the student into states the dataset never covered, compounding errors cause the policy to drift off-distribution, and performance degrades~\cite{Ross2011}.

DAgger~\cite{Ross2011} addresses this by iteratively rolling out the current student policy, querying the teacher at each visited state, and aggregating the new labels into the training set.
After $N$ iterations, DAgger provably finds a policy whose performance is bounded by $\epsilon_N + \mathcal{O}(1/N)$, where $\epsilon_N$ is the best achievable loss on the aggregated dataset.
Because the student drives data collection, the dataset gradually covers the states the student actually visits, and the compounding-error problem of \ac{BC} is largely eliminated.
DAgger is the distillation method used in DextrAH-G~\cite{Lum2024} and DextrAH-RGB~\cite{Singh2025}, which form the basis of the present work.

\subsection{Safety-Aware Distillation}%
\label{subsec:safe_dagger}%

DAgger has two practical drawbacks for dexterous manipulation.
First, it queries the teacher at every visited state, including states where the student already acts correctly, which is query-inefficient.
Second, it offers no mechanism to prevent the student from visiting dangerous states during its own rollouts; the student is allowed to fail freely as long as the teacher label is recorded afterwards.
For dexterous hands, where unsafe actions can damage finger joints or destroy the manipulated object, this is unsatisfactory.

SafeDAgger~\cite{Zhang2016} addresses both issues with a safety-dependent gating mechanism that decides, at every time step, whether the student or the teacher should execute the next action.
Whenever the student's proposed action deviates too far from the teacher's, the system hands control to the teacher, intercepting dangerous actions before execution.
This mixed-control scheme reduces both the number of teacher queries and the number of unsafe states visited during training.
The result is an implicit curriculum: early in training the teacher drives most of the trajectory, while later the student takes over in regions it has mastered.

\textbf{This thesis directly compares DAgger and SafeDAgger as the distillation algorithm} for a privileged dexterous grasping teacher.
To the best of the author's knowledge, no prior work has reported a head-to-head comparison of DAgger and SafeDAgger for arm-hand dexterous grasping with a vision-based student.

\section{Sim-to-Real Transfer through Domain Randomization}%
\label{sec:sim2real}%

Training entirely in simulation avoids the cost and risk of real-world data collection but introduces the \emph{sim-to-real gap}, referring to discrepancies between simulated and real physics, perception, and actuation.
\ac{DR} is the dominant strategy for closing this gap: simulation parameters are varied across training episodes so that the policy learns to be robust to a wide distribution of conditions~\cite{Tobin2017}.
The underlying hypothesis, illustrated in \cref{fig:domain_randomization}, is that if the real world falls within the distribution of randomized simulations, the policy will generalize at deployment time without fine-tuning.

\begin{figure}[htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.55\textwidth]{pics/domain_randomization_amplified}
    \caption{Illustration of domain randomization. A single calibrated simulation (red circle) may not match reality (green triangle), but a sufficiently wide distribution of randomized simulations (dashed ellipses) can encompass the real-world conditions, enabling zero-shot transfer. Figure from~\cite{Weng2019DR}.}
    \label{fig:domain_randomization}
\end{figure}

In dexterous manipulation, the most commonly randomized parameters are object properties (mass, friction, scale), actuator characteristics (joint stiffness, damping, effort limits), kinematic perturbations (link masses, center-of-mass offsets), and sensor noise (observation delays, measurement variance)~\cite{Handa2024, Andrychowicz2020, Peng2018}.
For contact-rich tasks such as grasping, friction and actuator gains are particularly influential because they directly determine whether a grasp is stable or slips.

Peng et al.~\cite{Peng2018} demonstrated zero-shot sim-to-real transfer of locomotion and manipulation policies through dynamics randomization, and the same idea underlies the in-hand reorientation results of Andrychowicz et al.~\cite{Andrychowicz2020}.
A drawback of manually selected randomization ranges is that they are difficult to calibrate: too narrow, and the real world falls outside the training distribution; too wide, and the task becomes so hard that learning fails.
\ac{ADR}~\cite{OpenAI2020} automates this trade-off by gradually widening the parameter ranges based on policy performance, producing an emergent curriculum from easy to hard.
Handa et al.~\cite{Handa2024} showed in DeXtreme that vectorized \ac{ADR} on top of GPU-accelerated simulation is sufficient to transfer in-hand manipulation policies trained purely in simulation to a real Allegro Hand without any motion-capture system.

\ac{ADR} is the strategy adopted for the teacher policy in this thesis (\cref{ch:experiments}), with a curriculum schedule custom-designed for the Agile Hand and \ac{FR3} hardware.

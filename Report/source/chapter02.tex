\chapter{Related Work}%
\label{ch:related_work}%

This chapter reviews prior work along the three axes most relevant to this thesis: dexterous grasping with multi-fingered hands, privileged teacher--student distillation and the DAgger family of algorithms, and sim-to-real transfer.
The goal is not to introduce concepts from first principles, which is the role of \cref{ch:methods}, but to position the present work against the existing literature and to make explicit which ideas it builds on and which it departs from.

\section{Dexterous Grasping with Multi-Fingered Hands}%
\label{sec:dexterous_grasping}%

Dexterous grasping with anthropomorphic hands has been studied for decades, but only recently have learned policies begun to outperform classical pipelines on unstructured object sets.
This section contrasts the two dominant paradigms and identifies why the learning-based approach is now the de facto standard for the kind of vision-driven grasping targeted in this thesis.

\subsection{Rule-Based and Analytical Approaches}%
\label{subsec:rule_based_grasping}%

Classical work on robotic grasping treats the problem as one of finding contact points on a known object surface that satisfy force closure, the property that the contact wrenches alone can resist arbitrary external loads~\cite{Bicchi2000, Ferrari1992}.
Given a 3D object model and friction parameters, analytical planners search for contact configurations that maximize a quality metric such as the largest wrench ball inscribable in the \ac{GWS}~\cite{Ferrari1992}.
For parallel-jaw grippers in structured industrial settings these methods have been highly successful.
For multi-fingered hands, however, the search space grows to $\mathrm{SE}(3) \times \mathbb{R}^{n_h}$ for an $n_h$-jointed hand, and exhaustive planning becomes intractable~\cite{Bicchi2000}.

In practice, classical dexterous systems are organized as sequential pipelines, in which perception, grasp planning, trajectory generation, and post-grasp manipulation are engineered as independent stages with hand-tuned parameters per robot and object set~\cite{Bicchi2000, Siciliano2009}.
Most systems fall back on predefined grasp taxonomies, such as power, precision, or tripod grasps, with manually specified finger configurations for each object category, and on hand-coded \emph{manipulation primitives} for sub-tasks such as ``close fingers to power grasp'' or ``rotate object using thumb opposition''.
The fundamental limitation is the dependence on accurate models of object geometry, contact physics, and robot kinematics; whenever any of these models is wrong, as is unavoidable for novel objects, deformable materials, or worn hardware, the planned grasp fails~\cite{Tang2024}.
Errors compound across the open-loop pipeline, and the lack of closed-loop feedback between perception and action prevents recovery from perturbations.

A middle ground is occupied by data-driven grasp synthesis methods that retain an analytical objective but replace the search with learning.
DexGraspNet~\cite{Wang2023} generated 1.32 million grasps for over 5{,}000 objects using a differentiable force-closure estimator, and Wu et al.~\cite{Wu2022} demonstrated 86.7\% grasp success on novel objects with a learned generative model.
These approaches scale better than pure analytical planning but still produce \emph{open-loop} grasp poses; the resulting grasps must be executed by a separate, typically classical, controller and offer no closed-loop reactivity.

\subsection{Learning-Based Approaches}%
\label{subsec:learning_based_grasping}%

End-to-end \ac{RL} sidesteps the brittle hand-offs of the classical pipeline by jointly learning perception, grasp strategy, and reactive control~\cite{Tang2024}.
Kumar et al.~\cite{Kumar2016} were among the first to demonstrate model-based \ac{RL} for contact-rich manipulation on a 24-\acs{DoF} hand.
The landmark work of Andrychowicz et al.~\cite{Andrychowicz2020} at OpenAI then showed that a Shadow Dexterous Hand could learn to solve a Rubik's Cube through massive-scale \ac{RL} in simulation followed by sim-to-real transfer, establishing that learned dexterous manipulation is viable on real hardware even at extreme task complexity.

The introduction of GPU-accelerated simulators, in particular Isaac Gym~\cite{Makoviychuk2021}, then made it possible to run thousands of parallel environments on a single GPU and reduced training times from weeks to hours.
This enabled a wave of more focused dexterous-manipulation works.
Handa et al.~\cite{Handa2024} introduced DeXtreme, which combined vectorized \ac{ADR} with a vision-based pose estimator to achieve robust in-hand cube reorientation on a real Allegro Hand using only commodity hardware.
Petrenko et al.~\cite{Petrenko2023} proposed DexPBT, applying population-based training to discover both reward weights and hyperparameters automatically and producing diverse skills including bimanual coordination.
UniDexGrasp++~\cite{Wan2023} decomposed the problem into a learned grasp proposal stage followed by goal-conditioned \ac{RL} execution, achieving generalization to novel objects beyond what analytical planning can offer.

While much of this work targets in-hand manipulation with a fixed wrist, real applications require coordinated arm-hand control: reaching, grasping, and transporting an object as a single behavior.
DextrAH-G~\cite{Lum2024} addressed this with a 23-motor arm-hand system, combining geometric fabrics~\cite{Ratliff2023} as a structured low-level control layer with an \ac{RL} policy on top, achieving fast and safe dexterous grasping from depth images.
DextrAH-RGB~\cite{Singh2025} extended this line of work to RGB input and demonstrated the first successful sim-to-real transfer of an end-to-end RGB-based dexterous grasping policy, training a privileged \emph{Fabric-Guided Policy} (FGP) as the teacher and using photorealistic tiled rendering for distillation.
\textbf{This thesis takes DextrAH-RGB as its starting point but trains the teacher as a standard \ac{RL} policy without geometric fabrics}, accepting a less constrained controller in exchange for a substantially simpler training stack and the ability to use a custom dexterous hand asset (the Agile Hand on a Franka Research 3) inside Isaac Lab's direct environment API.

\section{Privileged Teacher--Student Distillation}%
\label{sec:distillation}%

A recurring challenge in simulation-based robot learning is that the most effective policies are trained with information that does not exist on a real robot: ground-truth object poses, contact forces, and full kinematic state.
Privileged \emph{teacher--student distillation} resolves this by training a teacher policy with full state access and then transferring its behavior to a student policy that operates from realistic, deployable observations such as RGB images~\cite{Hinton2015, Lum2024, Singh2025}.
The distillation step is itself an imitation-learning problem, but with a uniquely cooperative expert: the teacher is a trained policy that can be queried at arbitrary states and at arbitrary scale, which makes interactive algorithms such as DAgger viable in a way that they would not be with a human expert.

\subsection{Behavioral Cloning and DAgger}%
\label{subsec:bc_dagger}%

The simplest form of imitation learning is \ac{BC}, in which a policy is fit by supervised regression on a fixed dataset of expert state--action pairs.
Pomerleau's ALVINN~\cite{Pomerleau1988} pioneered this approach for autonomous driving.
\ac{BC} is straightforward but brittle: small deviations from the expert's state distribution lead the student into states the dataset never covered, the actions taken there compound the deviation, and the policy drifts off-distribution and fails~\cite{Ross2011}.

Ross et al.~\cite{Ross2011} introduced DAgger to break this feedback loop.
DAgger iteratively rolls out the current student policy in the environment, queries the expert for the correct action at each visited state, and aggregates the new labelled data into the training set before retraining.
After $N$ iterations, DAgger provably finds a policy whose performance is bounded by $\epsilon_N + \mathcal{O}(1/N)$, where $\epsilon_N$ is the best achievable loss on the aggregated dataset.
Because the student itself drives data collection, the dataset gradually covers the states the student actually visits, and the compounding-error problem of \ac{BC} is largely eliminated.
DAgger has become the default distillation method in privileged teacher--student pipelines for dexterous manipulation and is the algorithm used in DextrAH-G~\cite{Lum2024} and DextrAH-RGB~\cite{Singh2025}.

\subsection{Safety-Aware and Mixed-Control Variants}%
\label{subsec:safe_dagger}%

Standard DAgger has two practical drawbacks for dexterous manipulation.
First, it queries the expert at every visited state, including states where the student already acts correctly, which is wasteful when expert queries are expensive.
Second, and more critically, it offers no mechanism to prevent the student from visiting dangerous states during its own rollouts; the student is allowed to fail freely as long as the expert label is recorded afterwards.
For dexterous hands, where unsafe actions can damage finger joints or destroy the manipulated object, this is unsatisfactory.

Zhang and Cho~\cite{Zhang2016} proposed SafeDAgger, which trains a binary safety classifier alongside the student policy.
The classifier predicts, for each visited state, whether the student's action is close enough to the expert's to be considered safe.
Whenever the classifier flags the student as unsafe, the system hands control to the expert, and only returns control to the student when the classifier predicts safety again.
This mixed-control scheme reduces the number of expert queries and, because dangerous student actions are intercepted before execution, also reduces the number of unsafe states the system actually visits during training.
The result is an implicit curriculum: early in training the expert drives most of the trajectory and the student learns from corrections in safe regimes, while later the student takes over in regions it has mastered.

DexSafeDagger~\cite{ChiZhang2026}, an unpublished extension developed by the advisor of this thesis, replaces the binary action-disagreement classifier of SafeDAgger with a learned Q-value-based risk predictor.
The teacher's RL-trained Q-function is reused at distillation time to estimate the long-term risk of each visited state, and the teacher takes over not only on action disagreement but also when the predicted Q-value indicates that the state is likely to lead to failure.
This is particularly relevant for dexterous grasping, where many unsafe configurations (object slipping, finger jam, near-singular wrist poses) are not obviously unsafe from action disagreement alone but are clearly unsafe from a value-function perspective.

A complementary line of work attacks the limited performance ceiling of pure imitation rather than its safety properties.
Huang et al.~\cite{Huang2025} introduced IBORL, in which an imitation-bootstrapped student is fine-tuned with online \ac{RL} so that the final policy can surpass the teacher.
Such methods are orthogonal to the DAgger variants discussed above and could in principle be combined with them, but they fall outside the scope of this thesis, which is concerned exclusively with the distillation step.

\textbf{This thesis directly compares vanilla DAgger and SafeDAgger as the distillation algorithm} for a privileged dexterous grasping teacher, taking inspiration from DexSafeDagger for the safety-classifier design but without the Q-value extension.
To the best of the author's knowledge, no prior work has reported a head-to-head comparison of DAgger and SafeDAgger for arm-hand dexterous grasping with a vision-based student.

\section{Sim-to-Real Transfer and Generalization}%
\label{sec:sim2real}%

Training entirely in simulation avoids the cost and risk of real-world data collection but introduces the \emph{sim-to-real gap}: the discrepancy between simulated and real physics, perception, and actuation.
Two broad strategies exist for closing this gap, and modern dexterous manipulation systems generally combine both.

\subsection{Domain Randomization and Curricula}%
\label{subsec:dr}%

The most widely adopted strategy is \ac{DR}, in which simulation parameters such as friction, mass, lighting, sensor noise, and actuator delays are varied across training episodes so that the policy learns to be robust to a wide distribution of conditions~\cite{Tobin2017}.
The underlying hypothesis is that if the real world falls within the distribution of randomized simulations, the policy will generalize at deployment time without any fine-tuning.
Peng et al.~\cite{Peng2018} demonstrated zero-shot sim-to-real transfer of locomotion and manipulation policies through dynamics randomization, and the same idea underlies the in-hand reorientation results of Andrychowicz et al.~\cite{Andrychowicz2020}.

A drawback of plain \ac{DR} is that the randomization ranges must be chosen by hand: too narrow, and the real world falls outside the training distribution; too wide, and the task becomes so hard that learning fails.
\ac{ADR}~\cite{OpenAI2020} automates this trade-off by gradually widening the parameter ranges based on policy performance, producing an emergent curriculum from easy to hard.
Handa et al.~\cite{Handa2024} showed in DeXtreme that vectorized \ac{ADR} on top of GPU-accelerated simulation is sufficient to transfer in-hand manipulation policies trained purely in simulation to a real Allegro Hand without any motion-capture system.
\ac{ADR} is the strategy adopted for the teacher policy in this thesis (\cref{ch:experiments}), although the curriculum schedule is custom-designed for the Agile Hand and FR3 hardware.

\subsection{System Identification and Hybrid Approaches}%
\label{subsec:sysid}%

An alternative to randomizing over many possible worlds is to identify the parameters of the actual world.
\emph{System identification} fits a simulation model to data collected on the real robot, producing a single best-fit simulator instead of a distribution.
Lin et al.~\cite{Lin2025} recently introduced an automated real-to-sim tuning module that uses less than four minutes of real-world data to calibrate simulator parameters, substantially reducing the sim-to-real gap for bimanual dexterous manipulation on humanoid robots.
The current consensus in the dexterous-manipulation literature is that hybrid approaches, in which system identification fixes the gross parameters and moderate domain randomization absorbs the residual uncertainty, generalize better than either extreme on its own; this thesis follows the same hybrid philosophy.

The simulator itself is also a non-trivial design choice.
Isaac Gym~\cite{Makoviychuk2021} introduced end-to-end GPU rigid-body and articulated-body simulation, eliminating the CPU--GPU bottleneck that limited earlier RL pipelines.
Isaac Lab (formerly Orbit~\cite{Mittal2023}) builds on Isaac Sim and exposes two environment design paradigms, a manager-based API and a direct API.
This thesis uses the direct API (\texttt{DirectRLEnv}), which provides full transparency over the simulation loop and matches the paradigm used in the published DextrAH-RGB implementation.

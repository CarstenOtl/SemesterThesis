\chapter{Discussion and Conclusion}%
\label{ch:conclusion}%

This chapter interprets the experimental results of \cref{ch:experiments}, discusses the limitations of the present study, and outlines how the findings shape the direction of the subsequent master's thesis.

%% =========================================================================
\section{Discussion}%
\label{sec:discussion}%

\subsection{The Value of Online Distribution Correction}%
\label{sec:discuss_bc}%

The three-way comparison between BC, DAgger, and SafeDAgger provides a clear empirical answer to the question of whether online distribution correction is necessary for safe policy distillation.
BC achieves the highest grasp success rate (87.8\,\%), matching the teacher within 2 percentage points, but produces the highest unsafe-episode rate (41.8\,\%).
The checkpoint sweep further reveals that BC overfits after 50k iterations, with lift success peaking at 95.6\,\% before degrading to 85.6\,\% at 100k.
These results confirm that training exclusively on the teacher's state distribution yields high in-distribution accuracy but fails to produce robust behavior when the student acts autonomously.
Online methods (DAgger, SafeDAgger) sacrifice peak lift performance for robustness by exposing the student to its own imperfect trajectories during training.

\subsection{SafeDAgger as a Pareto Improvement over DAgger}%
\label{sec:discuss_safedagger}%

SafeDAgger (72.7\,\% \ac{GSR}) and DAgger (71.7\,\%) reach comparable task performance, recovering roughly 85\,\% of the teacher ceiling.
The safety picture, however, differs substantially.
In standalone evaluation, where the student acts autonomously without teacher intervention, SafeDAgger achieves a lower \ac{UER} (30.2\,\% vs.\ 32.7\,\%).
During training, the gap is more pronounced: SafeDAgger maintains an unsafe-episode rate of roughly 15--20\,\% while DAgger fluctuates between 30\,\% and 40\,\% (\cref{fig:run9_unsafe_rate}).
For a hypothetical online deployment, in which each unsafe rollout corresponds to a hardware event rather than a simulation reset, this difference would translate into roughly half as many damaging episodes for the same number of training steps.

The per-object failure mode decomposition reveals that SafeDAgger's advantage is most pronounced on collision-prone objects such as the basketball shoe and toy bagger, where DAgger's unrestricted exploration causes persistent contact with the table surface.
SafeDAgger's intervention mechanism intercepts these dangerous approach trajectories early in training, allowing the student to learn from corrected states rather than from the consequences of its own failures.

Within the regime studied, SafeDAgger matches DAgger on task performance while strictly improving training-time safety, making it Pareto-dominant.
This is consistent with the intuition that dexterous grasping has a large fraction of trivially safe states (free-space approach, wrist alignment) on which the safety filter incurs zero cost.

\subsection{The Role of Implementation Details}%
\label{sec:impl_details}%

The safety-threshold ablation shows that SafeDAgger's behavior depends on the choice of $\delta$, with $\delta = 2.0$ producing markedly cleaner intervention-rate decay than either $0.5$ or $3.0$.
The calibration from the L2 action-disagreement distribution (targeting 20--30\,\% initial intervention) provides a principled recipe that may transfer to other action spaces, though the specific value is platform-dependent.

\section{Limitations}%
\label{sec:limitations}%

The present study has several limitations that constrain the generality of its conclusions and that motivate the work planned for the master's thesis.

\paragraph{Simulation only.}
All teacher and student policies were trained and evaluated in Isaac Lab.
No real-robot rollouts were performed, and no system identification was carried out on the physical Agile Hand or the FR3.
Although the sim-to-real-aware ADR schedule is designed to narrow the sim-to-real gap, the actual gap on the physical platform is unknown.
The unsafe-episode rates reported in \cref{sec:distillation_results} are simulator-defined and should not be interpreted as predictions of hardware damage rates.

\paragraph{Single hand, single arm, single task.}
All experiments use the Agile Hand on the FR3 and target a single task (top-down grasping of rigid objects from a flat surface).
The conclusions about SafeDAgger's safety advantage may or may not transfer to other hands (e.g., the Allegro or Shadow hands), other arms, or tasks that involve in-hand reorientation, regrasping, or tool use.

\paragraph{Direct action-disagreement gating instead of a learned safety classifier.}
The SafeDAgger variant used in this thesis gates teacher intervention on the L2 distance between the student's and teacher's actions, rather than on a learned binary safety classifier as in the original Zhang and Cho formulation~\cite{Zhang2016}.
This choice is justified by the fact that the teacher is queried at every step, which makes a separate classifier redundant.
It does, however, mean that the safety signal is purely action-based and does not incorporate value-based failure prediction.

\paragraph{No fabric-guided policies.}
The original DextrAH-RGB pipeline relies on fabric-guided policies to produce smooth, kinematically consistent action targets.
This component was deliberately omitted because of its implementation complexity, and the standard PPO actor was used instead.
Adding fabrics would likely improve the smoothness of the teacher's action distribution and indirectly improve distillation quality.

\section{Conclusion}%
\label{sec:conclusion_sec}%

This semester thesis presented a privileged teacher--student distillation pipeline for dexterous grasping with a custom 23-degree-of-freedom Agile Hand and Franka Research~3 platform.
Through a three-way comparison of BC, DAgger, and SafeDAgger, we validate that safety-aware distillation effectively reduces unsafe episodes during training while matching the task performance of vanilla DAgger.
SafeDAgger's advantage is robust across evaluation objects and failure modes, with the largest safety margins on collision-prone geometries.
A clear limitation is that the student's performance is largely constrained by the teacher's capability: the teacher stalls at ADR level~14, and all student policies recover at most 85\,\% of this ceiling.
Improving the teacher through structured low-level control is therefore a prerequisite for further progress.

\section{Future Work}%
\label{sec:future_work}%

\paragraph{Real-robot validation.}
The most important next step is to evaluate the SafeDAgger student on the physical FR3 and Agile Hand, both to measure the true sim-to-real gap and to test whether the in-training unsafe-episode reduction observed in simulation translates into fewer hardware-damaging events on the real platform.
This step also requires a basic system identification of the Agile Hand actuators, which was deliberately deferred in the semester thesis.

\paragraph{Improving teacher quality through low-level control.}
The teacher policy in this work reaches ADR level~14 before training stalls, whereas DextrAH-RGB and DextrAH-G report substantially higher ADR progression.
A likely explanation is that these systems use Fabric-Guided Policies (FGPs), which offload collision avoidance and palm-alignment constraints to a geometric-fabrics controller rather than learning them through reward shaping.
The RL policy then operates in a reduced, kinematically consistent action space rather than commanding raw joint positions.
DextrAH-RGB further reduces the hand's action space through Hand-PCA components derived from teleoperation data, which constrains the Allegro Hand to anthropomorphic synergies and simplifies exploration.
Integrating an FGP or similar structured low-level controller for the Agile Hand is a promising direction for improving ADR consistency and teacher quality, and would make the reward terms for collision avoidance and palm alignment ($r_{\text{palm}}$, $r_{\text{action}}$) redundant.
Since the student's performance is fundamentally bounded by the teacher's capability, a stronger teacher trained at higher ADR levels would directly improve distillation outcomes.
DextrAH-RGB, for instance, distills at maximum ADR, which ensures robust zero-shot sim-to-real transfer -- a property that the current pipeline does not yet achieve.

\paragraph{Scaling the distillation pipeline.}
DextrAH-RGB achieves stronger distillation results in part through longer training schedules, more parallel environments, and a multi-GPU setup.
The current pipeline is limited to a single RTX~4090, which constrains both the number of environments and the RGB rendering resolution ($320 \times 240$ at 30\,fps).
Higher-resolution images may improve the student's object localization and grasp accuracy, but would require substantially more GPU memory.
Exploring multi-GPU distillation or more memory-efficient rendering backends could unlock this trade-off.
On the architecture side, the ResNet-18 backbone with two removed layers and the two-layer cross-attention transformer were adopted from DextrAH-RGB without modification.
Ablations on the vision backbone (e.g., stronger feature extractors, different layer cutoffs), transformer depth, and hidden dimensions may yield better visual representations, particularly for the auxiliary object-position prediction task.

\paragraph{Stronger safety signals.}
The action-disagreement gate used in this thesis is the simplest reasonable implementation of safety-aware distillation.
Two extensions are natural candidates: (i)~replacing the L2 gate with a learned binary safety classifier in the spirit of the original SafeDAgger formulation, and (ii)~incorporating Q-value-based failure prediction as proposed in DexSafeDagger~\cite{ChiZhang2026}, which couples the safety decision to the critic of the privileged teacher and is therefore expected to be more selective in identifying genuinely unsafe states.

This chapter describes the experimental realization of the methods introduced in \cref{ch:methods}.

\subsection{Teacher Policy Training}%

\subsubsection{Simulation Environment}%
\label{sec:sim_env}%

All training and distillation is performed in NVIDIA Isaac Lab~\cite{Mittal2023}, a GPU-accelerated simulation framework built on Isaac Sim and PhysX~5.
The simulation runs at 120\,Hz with a policy control frequency of 60\,Hz.
Environments are implemented using the direct API (\texttt{DirectRLEnv}), following the same paradigm as DextrAH-RGB~\cite{Singh2025}.

\subsubsection{Hardware Platform}%
\label{sec:hardware}%

The robotic platform consists of a \ac{FR3} 7-\acs{DoF} torque-controlled arm equipped with an Agile Hand (16 actuated \acs{DoF}, 5 fingers, manufactured by Agile Robots SE), forming a 23-\acs{DoF} arm--hand system.
All joints are controlled via position targets with simulated PD controllers.
Each finger has MCP pitch ($0\degree$--$75\degree$), MCP yaw ($\pm 15\degree$), PIP ($0\degree$--$85\degree$), and DIP ($0\degree$--$85\degree$, coupled to PIP) joints; the opposing thumb adds a rotation \acs{DoF} ($60\degree$ range) at a significantly lower velocity ($\approx$12\,deg/s), which is a key constraint for sim-to-real transfer (\cref{sec:sim2real_curriculum}).
The arm and hand are combined into a single articulated asset in \ac{USD} format.
\cref{fig:hardware_platform} shows the physical Agile Hand and the assembled system as rendered in Isaac Sim.
The observation split between actor, critic, and student is described in \cref{tab:ac_observations} and \cref{sec:obs_space}.

\subsubsection{Reward Design}%
\label{sec:reward_implementation}%

The reward function follows the structure defined in \cref{eq:reward}.
\cref{tab:reward_terms} lists every term used in the final teacher training configuration, along with its functional form, weight, and sign.
Weights marked with a star are modulated by \ac{ADR} over the course of training (\cref{sec:adr_implementation}).

\begin{table}[htb]
    \centering
    \caption{Reward terms for teacher policy training. $d$ denotes hand-to-object distance, $\theta$ the palm alignment angle, $n_c$ the number of finger contacts, $m_c$ a binary contact mask, $\Delta a$ the action delta, and $\dot{q}$ joint velocities. Weights marked $\star$ are ADR-modulated; ranges give the endpoints.}
    \label{tab:reward_terms}
    \small
    \begin{tabular}{lp{3.2cm}lc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Term} & \textbf{Purpose} & \textbf{Form} & \textbf{Weight} \\
        \midrule
        $r_{\text{hand}}$    & Hand-to-object approach         & $w \cdot e^{-\alpha d}$             & $+4.0$ \\
        $r_{\text{palm}}$    & Palm alignment                  & $-w \cdot \theta^2$                 & $+0.7$ \\
        $r_{\text{curl}}$    & Finger curl penalty             & $-w \cdot \|q - q_{\text{ref}}\|^2$ & $0.3 \to 0.8^{\star}$ \\
        \midrule
        $r_{\text{contact}}$ & Hand-object contact             & $w \cdot n_c$                       & $+3.0$ \\
        $r_{\text{grasp}}$   & Thumb + finger in contact       & $w \cdot \mathbb{1}[\text{thumb} \wedge \text{finger}]$ & $+3.0$ \\
        $r_{\text{lift}}$    & Lift after stable contact       & $w \cdot e^{-\alpha |z - z_g|} \cdot m_c$ & $40 \to 30^{\star}$ \\
        $r_{\text{goal}}$    & Object-to-goal distance         & $w \cdot e^{-\alpha d_g} \cdot m_c$ & $+40.0$ \\
        $r_{\text{time}}$    & Proximity time bonus            & $w \cdot \mathbb{1}[d < 0.2]$       & $+0.005$ \\
        $r_{\text{success}}$ & Goal region bonus               & $w \cdot \mathbb{1}[\text{at goal}]$ & $+20.0$ \\
        \midrule
        $r_{\text{action}}$  & Action rate penalty             & $-w \cdot \|\Delta a\|^2$           & $0.01$ \\
        $r_{\text{vel}}$     & Joint velocity penalty          & $-w \cdot \|\dot{q}\|^2$           & $5 \!\cdot\! 10^{-4}$ \\
        $r_{\text{term}}$    & Early termination penalty       & $-w \cdot \mathbb{1}[\text{no contact}]$ & $1.0$ \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The lift and goal terms are gated by a contact mask $m_c$, ensuring the policy only receives reward for lifting when actually grasping the object rather than accidentally launching it.
Hand joints are penalized $1.5\times$ more than arm joints for action rate and $3\times$ more for joint velocity, encouraging smooth finger motions.

\subsubsection{Action Space}%
\label{sec:action_space}%

The action space is 23-dimensional, consisting of joint position targets for all 7 arm joints and 16 hand joints.
Actions are scaled from the policy's normalized output $a_t \in [-1, 1]^{23}$ to absolute joint position targets using per-joint scaling factors.
No geometric fabrics or motion primitives are used; the policy directly commands joint positions, which distinguishes this work from the FGP-based approach in DextrAH-RGB~\cite{Singh2025}.

\subsubsection{Episode Structure}%
\label{sec:episode_structure}%

Each episode has a maximum duration of 10\,s (600 steps at 60\,Hz) for teacher training and 5\,s (300 steps) for student distillation.
At the start of each episode, the object is placed on a table surface with randomized position and orientation, and the robot is initialized to a fixed home configuration above the object.
Episodes terminate at
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:termination}
    T = \min\left\{t \;\middle|\; \mathbb{I}_{\text{unsafe}}(s_t) = 1 \;\lor\; t \geq T_{\max}\right\},
\end{equation}
%
where $T_{\max} = 10\,\mathrm{s}$ and $\mathbb{I}_{\text{unsafe}}$ is the indicator of any early-termination condition in \cref{tab:terminations}.
These conditions cover both task failures (object escape, hand drift) and unsafe robot states (palm flip, table collision, numerical instability), and are reused as unsafe-event signals for SafeDAgger's intervention logic during distillation.
Early terminations before any object contact incur the $r_{\text{term}}$ penalty from \cref{tab:reward_terms}.

\begin{table}[htb]
    \centering
    \caption{Episode termination conditions. All early terminations mark the state as unsafe; penalty-eligible conditions additionally trigger $r_{\text{term}}$ when they occur before any object contact.}
    \label{tab:terminations}
    \small
    \begin{tabular}{lp{7.5cm}c}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Condition} & \textbf{Trigger} & \textbf{Penalty} \\
        \midrule
        \texttt{object\_escape\_xy}    & Object leaves the $\pm0.25\,\mathrm{m}\times\pm0.4\,\mathrm{m}$ workspace rectangle & \checkmark \\
        \texttt{object\_too\_low}      & Object falls below the table surface ($z < 0.2\,\mathrm{m}$) & \checkmark \\
        \texttt{hand\_too\_far}        & Hand workspace body leaves a $0.35\,\mathrm{m}$-inflated workspace box & \checkmark \\
        \texttt{hand\_too\_close}      & Any fingertip or palm comes within $1\,\mathrm{cm}$ of the table surface & -- \\
        \texttt{palm\_flip}            & Palm direction deviates more than $108^{\circ}$ from the target down-direction & \checkmark \\
        \texttt{arm\_table\_contact}   & Any arm link contacts the table & -- \\

\subsubsection{Network Architecture}%
\label{sec:teacher_network}%

The teacher policy uses an \ac{LSTM}-based recurrent network implemented in rl\_games.
The actor consists of an MLP encoder followed by an LSTM layer with 1024 hidden units, followed by an output head mapping to the 23-dimensional action space.
The critic has an identical architecture but receives the larger critic observation.

\subsubsection{Training Configuration}%
\label{sec:training_config}%

The teacher is trained with \ac{PPO} using a learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-4}$, clip parameter $\epsilon = 0.2$, horizon length 16~steps, discount $\gamma = 0.99$, GAE $\lambda = 0.95$, and minibatch size~2048 across 1{,}024 parallel environments for up to 33{,}000 epochs.
Training is performed headless on a single NVIDIA GPU.

\subsubsection{Sim-to-Real Curriculum}%
\label{sec:sim2real_curriculum}%

To close the sim-to-real gap, a second-generation teacher (teacher~11) is trained from scratch with hardware-realistic constraints incorporated into the \ac{ADR} curriculum.
The corresponding training results are reported in \cref{sec:teacher_results}.

\paragraph{Curriculum design.}
The sim-to-real curriculum adds three categories of hardware constraints that are progressively introduced via \ac{ADR}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Finger actuator gains:} Per-joint-group stiffness and damping scales, starting at nominal simulation values and widening toward hardware measurements. Initial attempts with hardware-exact gain targets (e.g., MCP pitch stiffness scale down to 0.17) caused policy collapse at ADR levels 16--18 (run~1a); relaxed ranges of $[0.1, 1.0]$ proved stable.
    \item \textbf{Effort and velocity limits:} The thumb rotation velocity limit is ramped from 20\,rad/s (simulation) toward 0.21\,rad/s ($\approx$12\,deg/s, hardware limit). Arm effort limits are reduced toward FR3 factory specs (87\,Nm for joints~1--4, 12\,Nm for joints~5--7).
    \item \textbf{Reward rebalancing:} The object-to-goal weight was doubled ($20 \to 40$) and a success bonus ($w = 10$) was added, since the original reward balance caused the policy to optimize for holding objects rather than delivering them to the goal height (run~1d plateau at in-success-region 0.25--0.35).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Student Policy Distillation}%
\label{sec:student_implementation}%


\subsubsection{Student Observation Space}%
\label{sec:student_obs}%

The student replaces the teacher's privileged object state with RGB image observations.
The student observation consists of:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Proprioceptive state:} Joint positions (23D), joint velocities (23D), hand position (3D), hand velocity (3D), object goal position (3D), and previous action (23D).
    \item \textbf{Visual input:} Simulated stereo RGB images at $320 \times 240$ resolution from wrist-mounted cameras.
\end{itemize}
Crucially, the student does \emph{not} receive the object position, orientation, scale, or contact information; these must instead be inferred from the visual input.

\subsubsection{Student Network Architecture}%
\label{sec:student_network}%

The student uses a vision--proprioception fusion architecture:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Visual encoder:} A ResNet-18 backbone processes each stereo image independently, producing a 32-dimensional feature vector per image.
    \item \textbf{Cross-attention fusion:} A cross-attention transformer module fuses the two stereo features into a single 64-dimensional visual embedding.
    \item \textbf{Policy head:} The visual embedding is concatenated with the proprioceptive state vector and fed into an \ac{LSTM} with 512 hidden units, followed by a linear output layer producing the 23-dimensional action.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{SafeDagger Distillation Configuration}%
\label{sec:distillation_config}%

The distillation follows the SafeDAgger procedure described in \cref{alg:safedagger}.
The safety policy uses the per-environment L2 action error as the intervention criterion: for each environment, the teacher takes over when $\|e_t\|_2 > \delta$.
The threshold $\delta$ was swept across multiple runs (see \cref{sec:ablation_threshold}); the final configuration uses $\delta = 2.0$.
The intervention rate $\beta_{\text{safe}}$ is computed as the fraction of environments where the threshold is exceeded.

Distillation uses Adam with a learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-4}$ and the combined loss from \cref{eq:total_loss} (auxiliary coefficient $\lambda_{\text{aux}} = 0.02$).
Training runs for 100{,}000 iterations across 24 parallel environments (3~per object) on an 8-object subset of the VisDex dataset, with stereo rendering enabled and domain randomization disabled.
Episodes last 10\,s, matching the teacher training configuration.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}%
\label{sec:eval_protocol}%

Three metrics are used to evaluate both teacher and student policies.
Each captures a different aspect of the pipeline: task completion, training-time safety, and hardware-relevant safety.

\begin{description}
    \item[\ac{GSR}] measures the fraction of episodes where the object is lifted above the table surface and held with stable contact for at least 0.5\,s (30 steps at 60\,Hz).
    The hold requirement prevents counting accidental upward launches as successful grasps.
    This is the primary task metric: a policy is useful only if it can reliably grasp and lift objects.

    \item[\ac{UER}] measures the fraction of episodes containing at least one early termination due to an unsafe event (\cref{tab:terminations}).
    This metric quantifies training-time safety, which is the central hypothesis of this thesis: SafeDAgger should reduce the number of unsafe episodes the student visits during distillation.

    \item[Real \ac{UER}] is the \ac{UER} excluding \texttt{physics\_instability} events (NaN joint states, velocity explosions), which are simulation-only artifacts that cannot occur on physical hardware.
    This metric provides a hardware-relevant safety estimate and is reported alongside the full \ac{UER} throughout the results.
    All \ac{UER} values reported in this thesis refer to the real \ac{UER} unless stated otherwise.
\end{description}

Standalone evaluation runs 480--640 episodes per configuration (60--80 per object) to produce stable metric estimates.

\section{Experimental Design}%
\label{sec:ablations}%

This section describes the distillation experiments and the rationale behind their design.
All experiments use the teacher from \cref{sec:teacher_training_setup} and the student architecture from \cref{sec:student_implementation}; each experiment isolates a single variable.

\subsection{Distillation Method Comparison}%
\label{sec:ablation_method}%

The central experiment compares three distillation methods: \ac{BC}, DAgger, and SafeDAgger.
All three use the same teacher, student architecture, loss function, and training budget; only the data-collection strategy differs.
\ac{BC} trains on a fixed offline dataset of teacher rollouts, DAgger iteratively collects data under the student's own policy, and SafeDAgger adds safety-aware gating (\cref{alg:safedagger}).
This three-way comparison isolates the effect of the distillation algorithm on both task performance (\ac{GSR}) and training-time safety (\ac{UER}).

\subsection{Safety Threshold Sensitivity}%
\label{sec:ablation_threshold}%

SafeDAgger's behavior depends on the safety threshold $\delta$, which Zhang and Cho~\cite{Zhang2016} did not systematically analyze.
This work sweeps $\delta$ across the values 0.5, 2.0, and 3.0, with $\delta \to \infty$ recovering vanilla DAgger as the upper bound.
Lower values trigger teacher intervention more aggressively, protecting the student from dangerous states at the cost of reduced autonomous exploration; higher values permit more student autonomy but weaken the safety benefit.
The sweep is designed to identify the threshold that best balances task performance against training-time safety for the 23-dimensional action space used here.

\subsection{Effect of Domain Randomization during Distillation}%
\label{sec:ablation_adr_distillation}%

All baseline distillation runs disable physics randomization (ADR level~0).
A follow-up experiment enables moderate randomization (ADR level~5) during distillation to test whether the student can tolerate physics variation while learning from a teacher trained to ADR~14.
Both SafeDAgger and DAgger are run under identical randomization to determine whether safety-aware gating degrades more or less gracefully than uniform-coverage imitation when the training distribution becomes harder.

\subsection{Effect of Environment Count}%
\label{sec:ablation_envs}%

Multi-object training requires sufficient parallel environments per object to produce stable per-object gradients.
The environment count is varied from 24 (3~per object) to 32 (4~per object) to quantify the effect on distillation quality, following the same principle that guided teacher training where scaling from 16 to 512 environments resolved gradient oscillation.

\section{Results, Graphs and Tables}%

\subsection{Teacher Training Results}%
\label{sec:teacher_results}%

The teacher policy was trained on 13 objects from the VisDex dataset using 1{,}024 parallel environments with the sim-to-real-aware \ac{ADR} curriculum described in \cref{sec:sim2real_curriculum}.
Training reached ADR level~14 after 33{,}000 epochs.
The resulting teacher achieves 85.8\,\% \ac{GSR} and 12.1\,\% real \ac{UER} at nominal (ADR-0) physics, demonstrating reliable multi-object grasping with the hardware-realistic actuator constraints.
This teacher is used for all distillation experiments reported below.

\subsubsection{Scaling Environments and ADR-Randomised Distillation (Runs~10--11)}%
\label{sec:run10_run11}%

Building on the run~9 baseline, two follow-up experiments push the SafeDAgger student further: run~10b raises the per-object environment count, and run~11a/b probes whether moderate physics randomisation during distillation is tolerable.
Both reuse the run~9 configuration (teacher~11, weighted L2 loss, $\delta = 2.0$, visdex\_top8, 100k iterations, 10\,s episodes) and modify a single variable.

\paragraph{Run~10b — 32 environments (4 envs per object).}
Increasing the parallel environment count from 24 ($3 \times 8$) to 32 ($4 \times 8$) yields the strongest student in this work: \textbf{79.2\,\% \acs{GSR}} on the 8-object evaluation set, reaching $92.3\,\%$ of the teacher's 85.8\,\% ceiling.
Real \acs{UER} (excluding physics instability) is essentially unchanged compared with run~9a (64.6\,\% vs.\ 62.7\,\% over all eval episodes).
The improvement is consistent with the multi-object teacher finding in \cref{sec:ablation_envs}: cleaner per-object gradients dominate the trade-off against per-iteration wall-clock cost.
A parallel ablation (run~10a) re-enabled visual and arm-pose randomisation at 24 environments and lost roughly 10~percentage points of training lift success compared with run~9a, indicating that visual diversity adds learning difficulty without matching the standalone evaluation conditions, and is best deferred to the sim2real curriculum.

\paragraph{Run~11a/b — Distillation under \acs{ADR}~5.}
All distillation runs up to this point disabled domain randomisation (ADR~0).
Run~11a (SafeDAgger) and run~11b (DAgger) repeat the run~10b configuration but enable \texttt{starting\_adr\_increments\,=\,5}, exposing the student to moderate friction, mass, and stiffness randomisation while the teacher (trained to ADR~14) still provides reliable supervision.
Both methods degrade compared with their ADR-0 counterparts, but the relative drop differs sharply: SafeDAgger falls from 79.2\,\% to 63.9\,\% \acs{GSR} ($-15.3$\,pp), while DAgger falls from 71.7\,\% to 48.8\,\% ($-22.9$\,pp).
The DAgger student's collision rate roughly doubles (12.5\,\% to 25.9\,\% of all evaluation episodes), whereas SafeDAgger's collision rate grows more modestly.
Within the time budget of this thesis, neither method recovers the ADR-0 performance under randomisation, suggesting that distillation under ADR requires longer schedules.
The asymmetric degradation, however, provides direct evidence for the SafeDAgger robustness hypothesis stated in \cref{sec:dagger_results}: query-aware imitation is less brittle to distribution shifts in the environment than uniform-coverage imitation.

\paragraph{Summary.}
The best student policy in this thesis is run~10b: SafeDAgger with 32~environments, $\delta = 2.0$, no \acs{ADR} during distillation.
It closes 92\,\% of the gap to teacher~11 on lift success and provides the strongest baseline for the eventual hardware deployment.
Run~11 indicates that ADR-randomised distillation is feasible but requires schedules longer than 100k iterations, and reinforces the SafeDAgger robustness advantage already visible in the run~9 head-to-head.

\section{Summary of Findings}%
\label{sec:results_summary}%

The key experimental findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{SafeDAgger and DAgger achieve comparable \ac{GSR}.}  SafeDAgger (72.7\,\%) and DAgger (71.7\,\%) reach similar grasp success rates, recovering roughly 85\,\% of the teacher ceiling (85.8\,\%). The best student (SafeDAgger with 32 environments) reaches 79.2\,\%, closing 92\,\% of the gap.
    \item \textbf{SafeDAgger produces safer students.}  The real \ac{UER} is 30.2\,\% for SafeDAgger vs.\ 32.7\,\% for DAgger at evaluation.  During training, the gap is more pronounced: SafeDAgger maintains $\sim$15--20\,\% real unsafe rate vs.\ $\sim$30--40\,\% for DAgger.
    \item \textbf{SafeDAgger degrades more gracefully under domain randomization.}  Under ADR-5 distillation, SafeDAgger drops by $-15.3$ percentage points vs.\ $-22.9$ for DAgger, indicating that safety-aware gating becomes more valuable as the training distribution becomes harder.
    \item \textbf{Safety threshold $\delta = 2.0$ provides the best balance.}  Among the tested thresholds (0.5, 2.0, 3.0), $\delta = 2.0$ allows the intervention rate to decay rapidly while maintaining teacher guidance on challenging states.
\end{enumerate}

Additional implementation findings related to LSTM checkpoint behaviour, physics stability, and teacher--student observation mapping are discussed in \cref{sec:impl_details}.

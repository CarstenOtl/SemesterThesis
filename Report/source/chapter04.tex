\chapter{Experiments and Results}%
\label{ch:experiments}%

This chapter describes the experimental realization of the methods introduced in \cref{ch:methods}.

\section{Experimental Setup}%
\label{sec:experimental_setup}%

All experiments are conducted in Isaac Lab 2.1.0~\cite{Mittal2023} (Isaac Sim 5.1.0) on a single NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU.
The simulation runs at 120\,Hz with a policy control frequency of 60\,Hz.
The robotic platform consists of a \ac{FR3} 7-\acs{DoF} arm equipped with a 16-\acs{DoF} Agile Hand (Agile Robots SE), forming a 23-\acs{DoF} arm--hand system controlled via joint position targets with simulated PD controllers (\cref{fig:hardware_platform}).
The arm and hand are combined into a single articulated \ac{USD} asset with convex-hull collision meshes.
The observation, action, and reward design are defined in \cref{sec:asymmetric_ac,sec:reward_structure}.

\subsection{Teacher Policy Training}%
\label{sec:teacher_training_setup}%

The teacher is trained with \ac{PPO} on 1{,}024 parallel environments with \ac{ADR} following the formulation in \cref{sec:adr_formulation}.
The teacher is trained on a set of 8 different objects (\cref{fig:training_objects}), spanning compact graspable shapes (teddy bear, closed fist) to thin and irregular objects (toy excavator, basketball shoe).
At episode reset, each environment is assigned one object uniformly at random, and the object's mass, friction, and scale are randomized within the \ac{ADR} ranges.
Each episode lasts up to 10\,s (600 steps) and terminates early if any unsafe condition from \cref{tab:terminations} is triggered.
These termination conditions are reused as unsafe-event signals for SafeDAgger's intervention logic during distillation.
An episode is considered successful if the object is lifted above the table and held for at least 0.5\,s (30 steps).
When the rolling success rate across all environments exceeds the \ac{ADR} threshold $\tau_{\text{ADR}} = 0.4$ (40\,\%), the randomization ranges are widened by one increment, progressively increasing the difficulty of the training distribution.

To encourage robust force closure, random external wrenches (forces and torques) are applied to the object whenever the hand is within $0.3\,\mathrm{m}$.
New perturbation directions are sampled every 120 steps ($\sim$1\,s), with magnitudes scaled by the object mass.
The maximum linear acceleration is ramped by \ac{ADR} from $0$ to $10\,\mathrm{m/s^2}$, exposing the teacher to progressively stronger perturbations as training advances.
During distillation and evaluation, \ac{ADR} is disabled and the wrench magnitude is zero, so the student never experiences external perturbations.
Training hyperparameters are listed in \cref{app:ppo_hyperparams}.

\subsection{Student Policy Distillation}%
\label{sec:student_implementation}%

The student replaces the teacher's privileged object state with stereo RGB images ($320 \times 240$ at 30\,fps) while retaining the proprioceptive inputs (joint positions/velocities, hand pose, goal position, previous action).
The student does \emph{not} receive the object position, orientation, scale, or contact information; these must be inferred from the visual input.
The visual encoder, cross-attention fusion, and LSTM policy head are described in \cref{sec:rgb_perception}.

Distillation follows the SafeDAgger procedure from \cref{alg:safedagger}, using Adam with a learning rate of $1 \times 10^{-4}$ and the combined loss from \cref{eq:total_loss}.
The teacher intervenes whenever the per-environment weighted L2 action disagreement (\cref{eq:safety_label}) exceeds the safety threshold $\delta$ (\cref{sec:ablation_threshold}).
Training runs for 100{,}000 iterations across 32 parallel environments (4 environments per object), which produces stable per-object gradients during online distillation.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}%
\label{sec:eval_protocol}%

Three metrics are used to evaluate both teacher and student policies.
Let $\tau \in \{1, \ldots, N\}$ index evaluation episodes and $t \in \{0, \ldots, T_\tau\}$ index time steps within an episode.
Evaluation episodes use a horizon of 10\,s and each configuration is evaluated over $N = 640$ episodes (80 per object).

\paragraph{Lift success (grasp with hold).}
Let $h_t^\tau$ denote the object height above the table at time step $t$ of episode $\tau$, and let $H$ be a lift threshold.
An episode is considered successful if the object remains above $H$ for at least $N_{\text{hold}}$ consecutive steps:
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lift_success}
    \mathbb{I}^{\tau}_{\text{lift}} = \mathbb{I}\!\left[\exists\, t : \forall\, k \in \{0, \ldots, N_{\text{hold}}-1\},\; h_{t+k}^{\tau} > H\right].
\end{equation}
%
In this work, $H = 0.15\,\mathrm{m}$ above the table surface and $N_{\text{hold}} = 30$ (0.5\,s at 60\,Hz).
The hold requirement prevents counting accidental upward launches as successful grasps.
The \ac{GSR} is the primary task metric:
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gsr}
    \text{GSR} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{\tau=1}^{N} \mathbb{I}^{\tau}_{\text{lift}}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Unsafe episode rate.}
An episode may terminate early due to one of four hardware-relevant failure modes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Harmful collision} ($m = \text{coll}$): any fingertip or palm body frame drops within 1\,cm of the table top, or any arm link contacts the table.
    \item \textbf{Object out of bounds} ($m = \text{obj}$): the object's XY position leaves the $\pm0.25\,\mathrm{m}\times\pm0.4\,\mathrm{m}$ area centered on the table surface, or the object falls more than 5\,cm below the table surface.
    \item \textbf{Palm flipped} ($m = \text{flip}$): the palm direction deviates more than $108^{\circ}$ from the downward-facing target direction.
    \item \textbf{Hand too far} ($m = \text{hf}$): the hand workspace body leaves the table footprint inflated by $0.35\,\mathrm{m}$, or rises more than $1.35\,\mathrm{m}$ above the table surface.
\end{itemize}
Let $\mathcal{M} = \{\text{coll}, \text{obj}, \text{flip}, \text{hf}\}$ denote this set of termination modes, and let $\mathbb{I}^{\tau}_{m} \in \{0,1\}$ indicate whether mode $m \in \mathcal{M}$ occurs in episode $\tau$.
An episode is unsafe if any mode is triggered:
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:unsafe}
    \mathbb{I}^{\tau}_{\text{unsafe}} = \mathbb{I}\!\left[\bigvee_{m \in \mathcal{M}} \mathbb{I}^{\tau}_{m} = 1\right].
\end{equation}
%
The \ac{UER} quantifies training-time safety:
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:uer}
    \text{UER} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{\tau=1}^{N} \mathbb{I}^{\tau}_{\text{unsafe}}.
\end{equation}
%
This definition excludes \texttt{physics\_instability} events (NaN joint states, velocity explosions), which are simulation-only artifacts that cannot occur on physical hardware.
All \ac{UER} values reported in this thesis refer to this hardware-relevant definition unless stated otherwise.
Full results including physics instability are provided in \cref{app:full_results}.

\paragraph{Failure-mode decomposition.}
Each episode is assigned a single terminal mode $m^\tau \in \mathcal{M} \cup \{\text{timeout}, \text{success}\}$.
When multiple unsafe conditions trigger in the same time step, the first condition (by a fixed priority order) is recorded.
Per-mode rates are reported as:
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:failure_mode}
    \text{FailRate}(m) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{\tau=1}^{N} \mathbb{I}[m^\tau = m], \quad m \in \mathcal{M}.
\end{equation}
%
This decomposition identifies which failure categories each distillation method is most prone to.

\section{Ablation Studies}%
\label{sec:ablations}%

The following ablation studies are conducted to justify the pipeline design.
The first ablation (\cref{sec:ablation_threshold}) identifies a suitable SafeDAgger safety threshold $\delta$ via a sweep over the weighted L2 action-disagreement distribution (\cref{eq:safety_label}) of an uncorrected DAgger student, determining the operating point used as the SafeDAgger configuration in the subsequent comparison.
The second ablation (\cref{sec:ablation_method}) compares the three distillation variants \ac{BC}, DAgger, and SafeDAgger at this threshold.
All three variants use the same teacher, student architecture, loss function, and training budget; only the data-collection strategy differs.
The variants are evaluated in terms of lift-success growth, unsafe-episode rate, and intervention rate (defined as the fraction of time steps in which teacher takeover occurs, $\beta = \frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\mathbb{I}[g_t = 1]$, averaged over evaluation episodes) during training.
Since the \ac{UER} is the primary safety metric, not only the average rate across all test objects is reported but also a per-object, failure-mode decomposition to assess each method under different failure conditions.

\subsection{Safety Threshold Calibration}%
\label{sec:ablation_threshold}%

SafeDAgger's behavior depends on the safety threshold $\delta$, which controls how often the teacher is allowed to override the student's action.
Zhang and Cho~\cite{Zhang2016} selected a single value from the error distribution of an initial supervised policy, targeting approximately 20--30\,\% early intervention.
Faithfully adapting this recipe to dexterous grasping requires the distribution of the per-step disagreement $e_t$ (\cref{eq:safety_label}) measured on a student trained \emph{without} interventions.
If the reference distribution of $e_t$ is taken from a SafeDAgger run instead, the largest values are missing from it, because those are exactly the moments at which the teacher had already overridden the student.
This work therefore uses a pure DAgger baseline ($\delta \to \infty$, no interventions) as the reference distribution on which the sweep range is defined.

The DAgger run yields the empirical distribution of $e_t$ across training, which is used in two stages.
First, the averages of this distribution at different training phases define a meaningful range of candidate thresholds.
Thresholds well below the late-training average are exceeded by $e_t$ at almost every step throughout training, so the teacher overrides the student nearly always and SafeDAgger collapses toward \ac{BC} behavior.
Thresholds well above the early-training average are essentially never exceeded, even at the start of training when student-teacher disagreement is largest, so the teacher rarely intervenes and SafeDAgger becomes indistinguishable from DAgger.
Second, within this range the threshold is swept in fine increments to identify the best operating point empirically.
A few low-end anchors and the $\delta \to \infty$ DAgger endpoint are added to characterize the boundary regimes.
The resulting operating point is used for SafeDAgger in the method comparison of \cref{sec:ablation_method}; the sweep results are reported in \cref{sec:threshold_results}.

\subsection{DAgger vs.\ SafeDAgger vs.\ BC}%
\label{sec:ablation_method}%

The central experiment compares the three distillation methods at the best SafeDAgger threshold identified from the sweep (\cref{sec:ablation_threshold}).
\ac{BC} trains on teacher rollouts with the teacher always driving the environment, DAgger collects data under the student's own policy with no intervention, and SafeDAgger adds safety-aware gating (\cref{alg:safedagger}).
All three use the same teacher, student architecture, training budget, and evaluation protocol; only the data-collection strategy differs.

\subsection{Teacher Training Results}%
\label{sec:teacher_results}%

The teacher policy was trained on the 8 objects shown in \cref{fig:training_objects} using 1{,}024 parallel environments with a sim-to-real-aware \ac{ADR} curriculum.

The resulting teacher achieves 99.0\,\% \ac{GSR} and 12.2\,\% \ac{UER} at nominal (ADR-0) physics, demonstrating reliable multi-object grasping with the hardware-realistic actuator constraints.

\subsection{Safety Threshold Results}%
\label{sec:threshold_results}%

The weighted L2 action-disagreement distribution (\cref{eq:safety_label}) extracted from the DAgger baseline (\cref{fig:threshold_calibration}) sets the sweep range.
The distribution decays from an early-training mean of $\sim$3.2 to a late-training mean of $\sim$1.9, with percentiles bracketing a meaningful sweep range of $\delta \in [2.0, 4.0]$.
At each threshold in this range, the observed SafeDAgger intervention rate $\beta$ (\cref{fig:beta_sweep}) confirms the relative ordering from the disagreement distribution: lower $\delta$ produces higher and more persistent intervention, higher $\delta$ decays quickly toward DAgger behavior.

\subsection{Distillation Method Comparison Results}%
\label{sec:distillation_results}%

All distillation experiments share the same teacher (99.0\,\% \ac{GSR}, 12.2\,\% \ac{UER}), the same student architecture (ResNet-18 + cross-attention + LSTM-512), and the same loss (\cref{eq:total_loss}).
The primary three-way comparison uses 100{,}000 distillation iterations at 32 parallel environments (4 per object).
SafeDAgger uses the best threshold $\delta = 3.0$ identified in \cref{sec:threshold_results}.

\subsubsection{DAgger vs.\ SafeDAgger vs.\ BC}%
\label{sec:dagger_results}%

\Cref{tab:distillation_comparison} reports the evaluation metrics for \ac{BC}, DAgger, and SafeDAgger at $\delta = 3.0$.

    \centering
    \caption{BC vs.\ DAgger vs.\ SafeDAgger standalone evaluation (640 episodes, 32 environments). SafeDAgger uses the best threshold $\delta = 3.0$ from \cref{sec:threshold_results}. \acs{UER} excludes physics-instability events; \cref{app:full_results} reports the full breakdown.}
    \label{tab:distillation_comparison}
    \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
        \toprule

SafeDAgger at $\delta = 3.0$ is the strongest student on both axes.
It achieves 97.0\,\% \ac{GSR}, within 2 percentage points of the teacher ceiling (99.0\,\%), and a lower \ac{UER} (38.3\,\%) than both \ac{BC} (41.4\,\%) and DAgger (52.2\,\%).
The three methods exhibit qualitatively different failure profiles that reflect their training-time data-collection strategy.

Since the lift-success metric requires the object to be held above the threshold for at least 0.5\,s (30 consecutive steps), all reported \ac{GSR} values reflect genuinely sustained grasps, not brief accidental lifts.
\ac{BC} achieves 87.8\,\% \ac{GSR} but the highest \ac{UER} (41.4\,\%), consistent with the covariate-shift failure described in \cref{sec:distillation_framework}.

DAgger lets the student drive the environment with no teacher intervention, producing fully student-distributed training data but also exposing the student to dangerous trajectories that it has no incentive to avoid.
Its \ac{UER} is the highest of the three methods (52.2\,\%), dominated by object-out-of-bounds terminations (35.0\,\% of all episodes), reflecting aggressive grasping without recovery behaviour.

SafeDAgger sits between these two regimes: the teacher intervenes on states where the student's action disagreement exceeds $\delta$, keeping the training-time state distribution close to the teacher's while still allowing the student to practice autonomously elsewhere.
At $\delta = 3.0$, intervention is sparse but targeted at the states where it matters, yielding the best-of-both-worlds outcome: near-teacher task performance and the lowest unsafe-episode rate among the three distillation methods.

SafeDAgger reaches the highest \ac{GSR} (97.0\,\%), recovering about 98\,\% of the teacher ceiling, while DAgger reaches 83.4\,\%.
The largest per-category safety gap is in the object out-of-bounds rate, where DAgger's unrestricted exploration produces a 35.0\,\% failure rate versus 20.0\,\% for SafeDAgger.

\Cref{fig:training_curves} shows the training-time lift success and unsafe-episode rate for all three methods.
BC's lift curve starts at the teacher level and stays there throughout training, because the teacher drives the environment at every step and the metric reflects the teacher's performance rather than the student's.
SafeDAgger converges quickly to near-teacher lift, while DAgger exhibits more variability as the student drives the environment without intervention.
The unsafe-episode rate (\cref{fig:unsafe_over_training}) reveals the safety gap during training: DAgger maintains $\sim$25--35\,\%, SafeDAgger settles at $\sim$10--20\,\%, and BC tracks slightly below SafeDAgger at a level close to the teacher's own \ac{UER}, because the teacher acts at every step and the training metric reflects the teacher's safety rather than the student's.
Notably, the training-time \ac{UER} is substantially lower than the standalone evaluation \ac{UER} for all methods.
For SafeDAgger this is partly expected, as the teacher still rescues high-disagreement steps during training.
The remaining gap, which also affects DAgger where no teacher intervention occurs, is not fully explained; one contributing factor may be the training metric's 100-episode rolling window, which could smooth over per-object variability that the 640-episode standalone evaluation captures uniformly.

\subsubsection{Failure Mode Decomposition}%
\label{sec:safety_analysis}%

The three-way comparison reveals a clear ranking between task performance and training-time safety.
DAgger lets the student visit dangerous states freely and pays the highest evaluation \ac{UER} (52.2\,\%).
\ac{BC} maximizes in-distribution action accuracy but still produces an evaluation \ac{UER} of 41.4\,\%, because the student never practices recovery from its own mistakes during training.
SafeDAgger at $\delta = 3.0$ achieves the lowest evaluation \ac{UER} (38.3\,\%) while simultaneously reaching the highest \ac{GSR}, by intercepting the few high-disagreement actions that would otherwise push the student off-distribution.
The observed intervention rate is sparse throughout training (\cref{fig:beta_sweep}), so the student acts autonomously in the vast majority of environments while the teacher rescues only the most misaligned ones.

Beyond the best operating point, the threshold sweep (\cref{tab:threshold_sweep,fig:threshold_primary_sweep}) shows that SafeDAgger produces policies with a lower \ac{UER} than DAgger across the majority of swept thresholds.
This indicates that safety-aware gating yields safer student policies on average, not only at a single well-chosen threshold.

\Cref{fig:run14_per_object} reports the per-object lift-success and unsafe-episode rates across the threshold sweep.
The safety advantage of SafeDAgger is uneven across objects: the largest margins appear on collision-prone shapes (basketball shoe, toy excavator), where the teacher's interventions most often replace an overly aggressive student action, while compact objects (closed fist, mario) show a narrower method gap because all three methods already lift them reliably.
Per-object training dynamics and full failure-mode decompositions are provided in \cref{app:per_object_failure}.

\section{Summary}%
\label{sec:results_summary}%

The key experimental findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{SafeDAgger produces the best student.}  At $\delta = 3.0$, SafeDAgger reaches 97.0\,\% \ac{GSR} (about 98\,\% of the teacher ceiling of 99.0\,\%) and a 38.3\,\% evaluation \ac{UER}.  It outperforms both \ac{BC} (87.8\,\%, 41.4\,\%) and DAgger (83.4\,\%, 52.2\,\%) on task performance \emph{and} safety simultaneously.  Moreover, SafeDAgger produces lower \ac{UER} than DAgger across the majority of swept thresholds, indicating that safety-aware gating yields safer policies on average, not only at a single operating point.
    \item \textbf{The threshold-performance curve is non-monotonic and noisy.}  The sweep exhibits multiple local peaks and dips whose locations are likely sensitive to individual training seeds.  The optimal threshold cannot be determined analytically from the disagreement distribution alone; an empirical sweep is necessary.
    \item \textbf{The three methods produce qualitatively distinct failure profiles.}  \ac{BC} produces the highest collision and object-out-of-bounds rates at evaluation; DAgger visits dangerous states freely and accumulates the highest object-out-of-bounds rate (35.0\,\%); SafeDAgger at $\delta = 3.0$ sits between them with sparse, targeted intervention and the lowest \ac{UER} overall.
\end{enumerate}

Additional implementation findings related to physics stability and teacher--student observation mapping are discussed in \cref{sec:impl_details}.

\chapter{Experiments and Results}%
\label{ch:experiments}%

This chapter describes the experimental realization of the methods introduced in \cref{ch:methods}.
It begins with the hardware and simulation setup (\cref{sec:experimental_setup}), details the teacher and student policy implementations (\cref{sec:teacher_implementation,sec:student_implementation}), defines the evaluation protocol (\cref{sec:eval_protocol}), and presents results for both teacher training (\cref{sec:teacher_results}) and distillation (\cref{sec:distillation_results}).

\section{Experimental Setup}%
\label{sec:experimental_setup}%

\subsection{Hardware Platform}%
\label{sec:hardware}%

The robotic platform consists of a \ac{FR3} 7-\acs{DoF} arm equipped with an Agile Hand (also referred to as Tekken Hand), forming a 23-\acs{DoF} arm--hand system.

\paragraph{Franka Research~3.}
The \ac{FR3} is a torque-controlled collaborative robot arm with seven revolute joints and integrated joint torque sensors on every axis.
% PLACEHOLDER: Add FR3 payload, reach, repeatability specs. Cite Franka Emika datasheet.
The arm provides Cartesian impedance control capabilities, though in this work all joints are controlled via position targets with simulated PD controllers.
The factory torque limits are 87\,Nm for joints~1--4 and 12\,Nm for joints~5--7.

\paragraph{Agile Hand.}
The Agile Hand, manufactured by Agile Robots SE, is a 16-\acs{DoF} anthropomorphic robotic hand composed of five identical, modularly designed fingers with an aluminum open skeleton structure and injection-molded plastic shells.
The hand weighs 1.5\,kg and has 21~joints total, of which 16 are independently actuated (one coupled joint per finger).
Each finger has four joints: a metacarpophalangeal (MCP) pitch joint ($-4\degree$ to $80\degree$), an MCP yaw joint ($\pm 15\degree$), a proximal interphalangeal (PIP) joint ($-4\degree$ to $90\degree$), and a distal interphalangeal (DIP) joint ($0\degree$ to $90\degree$, coupled to PIP).
The opposing thumb adds a rotation \acs{DoF} with a range of $60\degree$.
The hand provides 10\,N active fingertip force, $360\degree$/s joint velocity, and EtherCAT-based real-time communication at 1\,kHz.
Joint torque and position sensors are integrated in every actuated joint, enabling active compliant control.
The combined arm--hand system provides 23 actuated \acp{DoF}: 7~arm joints plus 16~hand joints.

\subsection{Simulation Environment}%
\label{sec:sim_env}%

All training and distillation is performed in NVIDIA Isaac Lab~\cite{Mittal2023}, a GPU-accelerated simulation framework built on Isaac Sim and PhysX 5.

\paragraph{Manager-based architecture.}
Isaac Lab provides two paradigms for environment design: a \emph{direct} API and a \emph{manager-based} API.
This work uses the manager-based API, which structures the environment into modular managers for observations, actions, rewards, terminations, events (randomization), and curriculum.
This differs from the direct API used in the published DextrAH-RGB implementation~\cite{Singh2025}, but provides cleaner separation of concerns and easier integration of custom reward terms and \ac{ADR} schedules.

The simulation runs at 120\,Hz with a control frequency of 60\,Hz (decimation factor of~2).

\paragraph{Contact physics.}
The fingertip material uses static friction~2.0, dynamic friction~1.5 at nominal conditions, with the global friction combine mode set to \texttt{max} so that the higher rubber fingertip friction dominates at the contact interface.
Object materials use static and dynamic friction of~1.0.
These values are subject to \ac{ADR} during training (see \cref{sec:adr_implementation}).

The objects include: mario, milk pot, turtle candle holder, toy bagger, teddy bear, closed fist, basketball shoe, chicken head in car, elephant toy, train, homer, toy cow, and plane.
Object meshes are stored as individual \ac{USD} assets.

\subsection{Episode Structure}%
\label{sec:episode_structure}%

Each episode has a maximum duration of 10\,s (600 steps at 60\,Hz) for teacher training and 5\,s (300 steps) for student distillation.
At the start of each episode, the object is placed on a table surface with randomized position and orientation, and the robot is initialized to a fixed home configuration.
The arm is initialized above the object in a top-down approach pose to facilitate contact.

An episode terminates early if any of the following conditions are met:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The object leaves the workspace bounds (\texttt{object\_out\_of\_bound}).
    \item The hand moves too far from the object (\texttt{hand\_too\_far}).
    \item A harmful collision is detected (\texttt{harmful\_collision}).
    \item The palm orientation flips beyond a threshold (\texttt{palm\_flipped}).
\end{itemize}
Early terminations incur a penalty of $-1.0$ on the reward to discourage the policy from exploiting short episodes.

\subsection{Action Space}%
\label{sec:action_space}%

The action space is 23-dimensional, consisting of joint position targets for all 7 arm joints and 16 hand joints.
Actions are scaled from the policy's normalized output $a_t \in [-1, 1]^{23}$ to absolute joint position targets using per-joint scaling factors.
No geometric fabrics or motion primitives are used---the policy directly commands joint positions, which distinguishes this work from the FGP-based approach in DextrAH-RGB~\cite{Singh2025}.

\subsection{Reward Design}%
\label{sec:reward_implementation}%

The reward function follows the structure defined in \cref{eq:reward}.
\cref{tab:reward_terms} lists all reward terms, their formulations, and weights used in the final training configuration.

\begin{table}[htb]
    \centering
    \caption{Reward terms and weights for teacher training.}
    \label{tab:reward_terms}
    \begin{tabular}{llc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Term} & \textbf{Formulation} & \textbf{Weight} \\
        \midrule
        Hand-to-object distance & $\exp(-\kappa \cdot \|p_{\text{hand}} - p_{\text{obj}}\|)$ & 4.0 \\
        Palm direction alignment & $\max(0, \hat{n}_{\text{palm}} \cdot \hat{d}_{\text{obj}})$ & 0.5 \\
        Hand-object contact & $\min(n_{\text{contacts}}, n_{\max}) / n_{\max}$ & 8.0 \\
        Good grasp & $\mathbb{1}[\text{contact mask}] \cdot \text{grasp quality}$ & 6.0 \\
        Object-to-goal (lift) & $w_{\text{lift}} \cdot \exp(-\kappa_l \cdot |z_{\text{obj}} - z_{\text{goal}}|) \cdot \mathbb{1}[\text{contact}]$ & 1.0 \\
        Finger curl regularization & $-\|\boldsymbol{q}_{\text{fingers}} - \boldsymbol{q}_{\text{curl}}\|^2$ & 0.5 \\
        Action rate penalty & $-\|a_t - a_{t-1}\|^2$ (fingers $\times 2.5$) & 0.1 \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The reward design underwent extensive iteration.
Key design decisions include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Contact-gated lift reward:} The lift term is multiplied by a contact mask, ensuring the policy only receives lift reward when actually grasping the object, not when the object is accidentally launched upward.
    \item \textbf{Finger action rate penalty scaling:} Hand joints are penalized $2.5\times$ more than arm joints for jerky actions, encouraging smooth finger motions critical for stable grasps.
    \item \textbf{Finger curl regularization:} A weak penalty encourages fingers toward a pre-grasp curl pose, providing an inductive bias for power grasps without constraining the policy.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Network Architecture}%
\label{sec:teacher_network}%

The teacher policy uses an \ac{LSTM}-based recurrent network implemented in rl\_games.
The actor consists of an MLP encoder followed by an LSTM layer with 1024 hidden units, followed by an output head mapping to the 23-dimensional action space.
The critic has an identical architecture but receives the larger critic observation.

\section{Student Policy Implementation}%
\label{sec:student_implementation}%

\subsection{Student Observation Space}%
\label{sec:student_obs}%

The student replaces the teacher's privileged object state with RGB image observations.
The student observation consists of:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Proprioceptive state:} Joint positions (23D), joint velocities (23D), hand position (3D), hand velocity (3D), object goal position (3D), and previous action (23D).
    \item \textbf{Visual input:} Simulated stereo RGB images at $320 \times 240$ resolution from wrist-mounted cameras.
\end{itemize}
Crucially, the student does \emph{not} receive the object position, orientation, scale, or contact information---these must be inferred from the visual input.

\subsection{Student Network Architecture}%
\label{sec:student_network}%

The student uses a vision--proprioception fusion architecture:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Visual encoder:} A ResNet-18 backbone processes each stereo image independently, producing a 32-dimensional feature vector per image.
    \item \textbf{Cross-attention fusion:} A cross-attention transformer module fuses the two stereo features into a single 64-dimensional visual embedding.
    \item \textbf{Policy head:} The visual embedding is concatenated with the proprioceptive state vector and fed into an \ac{LSTM} with 512 hidden units, followed by a linear output layer producing the 23-dimensional action.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{SafeDagger Distillation Configuration}%
\label{sec:distillation_config}%

The distillation follows the SafeDAgger procedure described in \cref{alg:safedagger}.
The safety policy uses the per-environment L2 action error as the intervention criterion: for each environment, the teacher takes over when $\|e_t\|_2 > \delta$, with $\delta = 0.5$.
The intervention rate $\beta_{\text{safe}}$ (\cref{eq:beta_safedagger}) is computed as the fraction of environments where the threshold is exceeded.

Key distillation hyperparameters:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Optimizer:} Adam, learning rate $1 \times 10^{-4}$
    \item \textbf{Distillation iterations:} 100{,}000--500{,}000
    \item \textbf{Number of environments:} 8 (with cameras enabled)
    \item \textbf{ADR:} Disabled during distillation (\texttt{enable\_adr=False})
    \item \textbf{Arm randomization:} Disabled (\texttt{disable\_arm\_randomization=True})
    \item \textbf{Stereo rendering:} Enabled (\texttt{simulate\_stereo=True})
\end{itemize}

\section{Evaluation Protocol}%
\label{sec:eval_protocol}%

\subsection{Metrics}%
\label{sec:metrics}%

The following metrics are used to evaluate both teacher and student policies:

\begin{description}
    \item[\ac{GSR}:] Fraction of episodes where the object is lifted above the table surface and held with stable contact for at least 0.5\,s (30 steps at 60\,Hz). The hold requirement prevents counting accidental upward launches as successful grasps.
    \item[\ac{UER}:] Fraction of episodes containing at least one early termination due to an unsafe event. Unsafe events are classified into four categories: \texttt{object\_out\_of\_bound}, \texttt{hand\_too\_far}, \texttt{harmful\_collision}, and \texttt{palm\_flipped}.
    \item[\ac{TQR}:] For SafeDAgger, the fraction of time steps where the teacher provides the action (i.e., $D_\psi(o_t) < 0.5$). This quantifies the student's autonomy during distillation.
    \item[Imitation loss:] The L2 action error between student and teacher, averaged over the evaluation batch.
\end{description}

\subsection{Evaluation Procedure}%
\label{sec:eval_procedure}%

Teacher policies are evaluated using \texttt{eval\_teacher.py}, which supports both single-checkpoint and multi-teacher (per-object directory) evaluation.
Student policies are evaluated using \texttt{eval\_student.py}, which additionally reports per-object metrics, failure reason breakdown, and the hold-gated lift success criterion.

\section{Teacher Training Results}%
\label{sec:teacher_results}%

\subsection{Training Progression}%
\label{sec:training_progression}%

The teacher policy was trained on 13 objects from the VisDex dataset using 512 parallel environments.
Training proceeded in two phases:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Initial training (16 envs, runs 1a--1o):} A series of iterative experiments to resolve physics stability issues, reward shaping, and early termination handling. With only 16~environments across 13~objects ($\approx$1.2~envs per object), training exhibited high gradient variance and cyclic performance collapses.
    \item \textbf{Scaled training (512 envs, run~1p):} Resumed from the best checkpoint of the initial phase with 512~environments ($\approx$39~envs per object), yielding stable gradient estimates and consistent learning progress.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Ablation Studies}%
\label{sec:ablations}%

\subsection{Effect of Number of Training Environments}%
\label{sec:ablation_envs}%

A critical finding from the teacher training experiments is the importance of sufficient parallel environments for multi-object training.
With 16~environments across 13~objects, the policy exhibited cyclic performance collapses due to high gradient variance ($\sim$1.2~envs per object per batch).
Scaling to 512~environments ($\sim$39~envs per object) produced stable, monotonic learning progress and enabled the first successful ADR progression.
This suggests a minimum of $\sim$30 environments per object class is necessary for stable multi-object policy training.

\subsection{Effect of Early Termination Penalty}%
\label{sec:ablation_penalty}%

Without an early termination penalty, the teacher policy learned to exploit short episodes: minimizing total negative reward by terminating quickly rather than attempting to grasp.
Introducing a flat penalty of $-1.0$ per early termination eliminated this failure mode and was necessary for the policy to learn approach and contact behaviors.
A higher penalty of $-3.0$ was initially tested but was reduced because it caused the policy to avoid any behavior that risked termination, including the necessary approach phase.

\subsection{Effect of Teacher Strength on Distillation}%
\label{sec:ablation_teacher_strength}%

The comparison between ADR-5 and ADR-19 teachers (\cref{tab:distillation_results}) demonstrates that teacher policy quality is a primary bottleneck for distillation performance.
The stronger teacher produces more consistent demonstrations, leading to lower imitation loss and higher task success.
This aligns with the general finding in the imitation learning literature that demonstration quality matters more than demonstration quantity.

\section{Summary of Findings}%
\label{sec:results_summary}%

The key experimental findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Physics stability is the primary challenge} for training dexterous grasping policies with custom hand assets in Isaac Lab.  Actuator parameters (effort limits, damping) must be carefully tuned to prevent simulation instabilities at contact, which otherwise cause the policy to learn contact-avoidance behaviors.
    \item \textbf{Sufficient parallel environments are critical} for multi-object teacher training.  A minimum of $\sim$30 environments per object class is needed to avoid gradient oscillation and cyclic collapses.
    \item \textbf{Teacher policy quality is the primary bottleneck} for distillation.  The ADR-19 teacher (trained longer with more environments) produces a 40\% relative improvement in student lift success compared to the ADR-5 teacher.
    \item \textbf{SafeDAgger distillation produces functional grasping students} that can approach, contact, and lift objects from RGB input, achieving 87.5\% lift rate with the ADR-19 teacher after 100k distillation iterations.
    \item \textbf{Extended distillation is needed:} At 100k iterations, the student's intervention rate remains high ($\beta = 0.875$), indicating room for improvement with longer training.
\end{enumerate}

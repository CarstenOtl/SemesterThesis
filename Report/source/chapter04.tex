\section{Experimental Setup}%

\subsection{Hardware Platform}%
\label{sec:hardware}%

The robotic platform consists of a \ac{FR3} 7-\acs{DoF} arm equipped with an Agile Hand, forming a 23-\acs{DoF} arm--hand system.

\paragraph{Franka Research~3.}
The \ac{FR3} is a torque-controlled collaborative robot arm with seven revolute joints and integrated joint torque sensors on every axis.
% PLACEHOLDER: Add FR3 payload, reach, repeatability specs. Cite Franka Emika datasheet.
The arm provides Cartesian impedance control capabilities, though in this work all joints are controlled via position targets with simulated PD controllers.
The factory torque limits are 87\,Nm for joints~1--4 and 12\,Nm for joints~5--7.

\paragraph{Agile Hand.}
The Agile Hand, manufactured by Agile Robots SE, is a 16-\acs{DoF} anthropomorphic robotic hand composed of five identical, modularly designed fingers with an aluminum open skeleton structure and injection-molded plastic shells.
The hand weighs 1.5\,kg and has 21~joints total, of which 16 are independently actuated (one coupled joint per finger).
Each finger has four joints: a metacarpophalangeal (MCP) pitch joint ($0\degree$ to $75\degree$), an MCP yaw joint ($\pm 15\degree$), a proximal interphalangeal (PIP) joint ($0\degree$ to $85\degree$), and a distal interphalangeal (DIP) joint ($0\degree$ to $85\degree$, coupled to PIP).
The opposing thumb adds a rotation \acs{DoF} with a range of $60\degree$.
The hand provides 10\,N active fingertip force, $360\degree$/s finger joint velocity, and EtherCAT-based real-time communication at 1\,kHz.
The thumb rotation joint operates at a significantly lower velocity ($\approx$12\,deg/s), which is a key constraint for sim-to-real transfer (see \cref{sec:sim2real_curriculum}).
Joint torque and position sensors are integrated in every actuated joint, enabling active compliant control.
The combined arm--hand system provides 23 actuated \acp{DoF}: 7~arm joints plus 16~hand joints.
\cref{fig:hardware_platform} shows the physical Agile Hand and the assembled system as rendered in Isaac Sim.

\subsection{Simulation Environment}%
\label{sec:sim_env}%

All training and distillation is performed in NVIDIA Isaac Lab~\cite{Mittal2023}, a GPU-accelerated simulation framework built on Isaac Sim and PhysX 5.

\paragraph{Direct environment API.}
Isaac Lab provides two paradigms for environment design: a \emph{manager-based} API and a \emph{direct} API (\texttt{DirectRLEnv}).
This work uses the direct API, which provides full transparency over the simulation loop: observations, rewards, resets, and domain randomization are implemented explicitly in Python rather than delegated to abstracted manager modules.
This is the same paradigm used in the published DextrAH-RGB implementation~\cite{Singh2025} and enables fine-grained control over reward computation, \ac{ADR} scheduling, and physics parameter manipulation at each time step.

\subsection{Episode Structure}%
\label{sec:episode_structure}%

Each episode has a maximum duration of 10\,s (600 steps at 60\,Hz) for teacher training and 5\,s (300 steps) for student distillation.
At the start of each episode, the object is placed on a table surface with randomized position and orientation, and the robot is initialized to a fixed home configuration.
The arm is initialized above the object in a top-down approach pose to facilitate contact.

An episode terminates early if any of the following conditions are met:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The object leaves the workspace bounds (\texttt{object\_out\_of\_bound}).
    \item The hand moves too far from the object (\texttt{hand\_too\_far}).
    \item A harmful collision is detected (\texttt{harmful\_collision}).
    \item The palm orientation flips beyond a threshold (\texttt{palm\_flipped}).
\end{itemize}
Early terminations incur a penalty of $-1.0$ on the reward to discourage the policy from exploiting short episodes.

\subsection{Action Space}%
\label{sec:action_space}%

The action space is 23-dimensional, consisting of joint position targets for all 7 arm joints and 16 hand joints.
Actions are scaled from the policy's normalized output $a_t \in [-1, 1]^{23}$ to absolute joint position targets using per-joint scaling factors.
No geometric fabrics or motion primitives are used; the policy directly commands joint positions, which distinguishes this work from the FGP-based approach in DextrAH-RGB~\cite{Singh2025}.

\subsection{Reward Design}%
\label{sec:reward_implementation}%

The reward function follows the structure defined in \cref{eq:reward}.
\cref{tab:reward_terms} lists all reward terms, their formulations, and weights used in the final training configuration.

\begin{table}[htb]
    \centering
    \caption{Reward terms and weights for teacher training.}
    \label{tab:reward_terms}
    \begin{tabular}{llc}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Term} & \textbf{Formulation} & \textbf{Weight} \\
        \midrule
        Hand-to-object distance & $\exp(-\kappa \cdot \|p_{\text{hand}} - p_{\text{obj}}\|)$ & 4.0 \\
        Palm direction alignment & $\max(0, \hat{n}_{\text{palm}} \cdot \hat{d}_{\text{obj}})$ & 0.5 \\
        Hand-object contact & $\min(n_{\text{contacts}}, n_{\max}) / n_{\max}$ & 8.0 \\
        Good grasp & $\mathbb{1}[\text{contact mask}] \cdot \text{grasp quality}$ & 6.0 \\
        Object-to-goal (lift) & $w_{\text{lift}} \cdot \exp(-\kappa_l \cdot |z_{\text{obj}} - z_{\text{goal}}|) \cdot \mathbb{1}[\text{contact}]$ & 1.0 \\
        Finger curl regularization & $-\|\boldsymbol{q}_{\text{fingers}} - \boldsymbol{q}_{\text{curl}}\|^2$ & 0.5 \\
        Action rate penalty & $-\|a_t - a_{t-1}\|^2$ (fingers $\times 2.5$) & 0.1 \\
        \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The reward design underwent extensive iteration.
Key design decisions include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Contact-gated lift reward:} The lift term is multiplied by a contact mask, ensuring the policy only receives lift reward when actually grasping the object, not when the object is accidentally launched upward.
    \item \textbf{Finger action rate penalty scaling:} Hand joints are penalized $2.5\times$ more than arm joints for jerky actions, encouraging smooth finger motions critical for stable grasps.
    \item \textbf{Finger curl regularization:} A weak penalty encourages fingers toward a pre-grasp curl pose, providing an inductive bias for power grasps without constraining the policy.
\end{itemize}

\label{sec:teacher_network}%

The critic has an identical architecture but receives the larger critic observation.

\section{Student Policy Implementation}%
\label{sec:student_implementation}%

\begin{figure}[htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{pics/Student Training_pipeline}
    \caption{Student distillation pipeline using Online SafeDAgger. The frozen teacher policy provides expert actions $\boldsymbol{a}_T$ from noisy state observations, while the student policy produces actions $\boldsymbol{a}_S$ from stereo RGB images and proprioception. A learned safety policy $D_\psi$ evaluates the L2 action disagreement between teacher and student to determine whether the student may act autonomously or the teacher must intervene, controlled by a safety threshold~$\delta$.}
    \label{fig:student_training}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Student Observation Space}%
\label{sec:student_obs}%

The student replaces the teacher's privileged object state with RGB image observations.
The student observation consists of:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Proprioceptive state:} Joint positions (23D), joint velocities (23D), hand position (3D), hand velocity (3D), object goal position (3D), and previous action (23D).
    \item \textbf{Visual input:} Simulated stereo RGB images at $320 \times 240$ resolution from wrist-mounted cameras.
\end{itemize}
Crucially, the student does \emph{not} receive the object position, orientation, scale, or contact information; these must instead be inferred from the visual input.

\subsection{Student Network Architecture}%
\label{sec:student_network}%

The student uses a vision--proprioception fusion architecture:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Visual encoder:} A ResNet-18 backbone processes each stereo image independently, producing a 32-dimensional feature vector per image.
    \item \textbf{Cross-attention fusion:} A cross-attention transformer module fuses the two stereo features into a single 64-dimensional visual embedding.
    \item \textbf{Policy head:} The visual embedding is concatenated with the proprioceptive state vector and fed into an \ac{LSTM} with 512 hidden units, followed by a linear output layer producing the 23-dimensional action.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{SafeDagger Distillation Configuration}%
\label{sec:distillation_config}%

The distillation follows the SafeDAgger procedure described in \cref{alg:safedagger}.
The safety policy uses the per-environment L2 action error as the intervention criterion: for each environment, the teacher takes over when $\|e_t\|_2 > \delta$.
The threshold $\delta$ was swept across multiple runs (see \cref{sec:ablation_threshold}); the final configuration uses $\delta = 2.0$.
The intervention rate $\beta_{\text{safe}}$ (\cref{eq:beta_safedagger}) is computed as the fraction of environments where the threshold is exceeded.

Key distillation hyperparameters:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Optimizer:} Adam, learning rate $1 \times 10^{-4}$
    \item \textbf{Loss:} Weighted L2 with auxiliary object position loss (coefficient 0.02)
    \item \textbf{Distillation iterations:} 100{,}000
    \item \textbf{Number of environments:} 24 (3 per object, with cameras enabled)
    \item \textbf{Object set:} 8 objects (VisDex top-8 subset)
    \item \textbf{Episode length:} 10\,s (matching teacher training)
    \item \textbf{ADR:} Disabled during distillation (\texttt{enable\_adr=False})
    \item \textbf{Arm randomization:} Disabled (\texttt{disable\_arm\_randomization=True})
    \item \textbf{Stereo rendering:} Enabled (\texttt{simulate\_stereo=True})
\end{itemize}

\section{Evaluation Protocol}%
\label{sec:eval_protocol}%

\subsection{Metrics}%
\label{sec:metrics}%

The following metrics are used to evaluate both teacher and student policies:

\begin{description}
    \item[\ac{GSR}:] Fraction of episodes where the object is lifted above the table surface and held with stable contact for at least 0.5\,s (30 steps at 60\,Hz). The hold requirement prevents counting accidental upward launches as successful grasps.
    \item[\ac{UER}:] Fraction of episodes containing at least one early termination due to an unsafe event. Unsafe events are classified into five categories: \texttt{object\_out\_of\_bound}, \texttt{hand\_too\_far}, \texttt{harmful\_collision}, \texttt{palm\_flipped}, and \texttt{physics\_instability} (simulation-only artifacts such as NaN joint states or velocity explosions, not relevant for real-world deployment).
    \item[Real \ac{UER}:] The \ac{UER} excluding \texttt{physics\_instability}, representing only hardware-relevant unsafe events.
    \item[Imitation loss:] The weighted L2 action error between student and teacher, averaged over the evaluation batch.
\end{description}

\subsection{Evaluation Procedure}%
\label{sec:eval_procedure}%

Teacher policies are evaluated using \texttt{eval\_teacher.py}, which supports both single-checkpoint and multi-teacher (per-object directory) evaluation.
Student policies are evaluated using \texttt{eval\_student.py}, which additionally reports per-object metrics, failure reason breakdown, and the hold-gated lift success criterion.

\section{Teacher Training Results}%
\label{sec:teacher_results}%

\subsection{Training Progression}%
\label{sec:training_progression}%

The teacher policy was trained on 13 objects from the VisDex dataset using 512 parallel environments.
Training proceeded in two phases:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Initial training (16 envs, runs 1a--1o):} A series of iterative experiments to resolve physics stability issues, reward shaping, and early termination handling. With only 16~environments across 13~objects ($\approx$1.2~envs per object), training exhibited high gradient variance and cyclic performance collapses.
    \item \textbf{Scaled training (512 envs, run~1p):} Resumed from the best checkpoint of the initial phase with 512~environments ($\approx$39~envs per object), yielding stable gradient estimates and consistent learning progress.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{figure}[htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{plots/teacher_training_curve}
    \caption{Teacher policy training: mean episode reward (blue, left axis) and ADR level (orange, right axis) over training epochs. The first ADR increment occurred at epoch~8850. Reward decreases at higher ADR levels reflect harder physics conditions, not policy degradation.}
    \label{fig:teacher_training_curve}
\end{figure}

The policy reached ADR level~5 by epoch~11{,}124, demonstrating reliable grasping and lifting across all 13 objects.
A subsequent extended run with 1024 environments reached ADR level~19 after 20{,}000 epochs, producing a stronger teacher for distillation.

\subsection{Physics Stability Challenges}%
\label{sec:physics_challenges}%

A central challenge in training was achieving stable contact physics for the Agile Hand.
Resolving these issues required 15 iterative runs (1a--1o) before stable training was possible.
The key failure modes, their root causes, and resolutions are summarized in \cref{tab:physics_challenges}.

\section{Ablation Studies}%
\label{sec:ablations}%

\subsection{Effect of Number of Training Environments}%
\label{sec:ablation_envs}%

A critical finding from the teacher training experiments is the importance of sufficient parallel environments for multi-object training.
With 16~environments across 13~objects ($\sim$1.2~envs per object per batch), the policy exhibited cyclic performance collapses: it would learn to grasp one object class, then catastrophically forget others as gradient updates were dominated by whichever objects happened to appear in the current batch (run~1o achieved only $\sim$2.4\% oscillating lift success).
Scaling to 512~environments ($\sim$39~envs per object) in run~1p, resumed from the best checkpoint of run~1o, immediately produced stable, monotonic learning progress and enabled the first successful ADR progression (ADR level~5 by epoch~11{,}124).
The subsequent sim2real teacher (teacher~11) used 1{,}024~environments ($\sim$79~envs per object) to further improve training stability.
A brief experiment with 2{,}048~environments across 2~GPUs (exp\_03, run~1e) showed that multi-GPU training was $2\times$ slower than single-GPU due to inter-GPU synchronization overhead, and was not pursued further.

These results suggest a minimum of $\sim$30--40 environments per object class is necessary for stable multi-object policy training with recurrent architectures.

\subsection{Effect of Early Termination Penalty}%
\label{sec:ablation_penalty}%

Without an early termination penalty, the teacher policy learned to exploit short episodes: minimizing total negative reward by terminating quickly rather than attempting to grasp.
Introducing a flat penalty of $-1.0$ per early termination eliminated this failure mode and was necessary for the policy to learn approach and contact behaviors.
A higher penalty of $-3.0$ was initially tested but was reduced because it caused the policy to avoid any behavior that risked termination, including the necessary approach phase.

\subsection{Effect of Teacher Strength on Distillation}%
\label{sec:ablation_teacher_strength}%

The comparison between ADR-5 and ADR-19 teachers (\cref{tab:distillation_summary}) demonstrates that teacher policy quality is a primary bottleneck for distillation performance.
The stronger teacher produces more consistent demonstrations, leading to lower imitation loss and higher task success.
This aligns with the general finding in the imitation learning literature that demonstration quality matters more than demonstration quantity.
\cref{fig:teacher_vs_student} illustrates the performance gap between teacher~11 and its best students from run~9, highlighting the remaining distillation loss in \ac{GSR} and the increase in \ac{UER}.

\subsection{LSTM Checkpoint Resume for ADR Curriculum}%
\label{sec:ablation_lstm_resume}%

An important negative finding from the sim-to-real curriculum experiments (exp\_03) is that \textbf{resuming LSTM-based teacher policies from checkpoints does not work reliably for continued ADR training}.
Three independent runs (1a--1c) resumed from the ADR-19 checkpoint and all degraded at ADR levels 16--18, with lift success dropping from $\sim$70\% to $\sim$15--40\%.

Investigation of the rl\_games checkpoint format confirmed the root cause: while model weights, optimizer state, and running statistics are saved, \textbf{LSTM hidden states are reset to zero on load}.
The LSTM must rebuild its temporal representations from scratch.
At low ADR levels this is feasible (the task is easy enough), but at high ADR levels the difficulty margin is too thin for a cold-started LSTM to re-learn temporal context while also handling physics randomization.
The original training succeeded because the LSTM built context \emph{continuously} through ADR levels 0$\to$19.

This finding motivated training the sim-to-real teacher from scratch (run~1d) rather than fine-tuning the existing checkpoint, incorporating all sim-to-real curriculum terms from the beginning.

\subsection{Safety Threshold Sensitivity}%
\label{sec:ablation_threshold}%

A notable gap in the original SafeDAgger paper~\cite{Zhang2016} is the lack of a systematic sensitivity analysis for the safety threshold $\delta$.
Zhang and Cho selected a single threshold ($\tau = 0.0025$ in their steering-angle domain) based on the error distribution of the initial supervised policy and did not vary it.
This work provides an implicit threshold sweep through runs conducted with different $\delta$ values: 0.5 (runs~1c, 3a, 5a), 0.665 (run~6a, scaled by $\sqrt{23/13}$ for the 23-dimensional action space), 2.0 (runs~8a, 9a), and 3.0 (run~7a).

Lower $\delta$ values trigger teacher intervention more aggressively (more protective, fewer student-executed actions), while higher values allow the student to act autonomously in more states (less protective, more exploration).
The extreme case $\delta \to \infty$ recovers vanilla DAgger (no intervention).

\cref{tab:threshold_sweep} summarizes the threshold sensitivity results.
All runs use teacher~11, weighted L2 loss, and 24~environments.
Runs~7a and 8a used 8 objects but had the one-hot index bug, so their absolute performance is lower; the comparison between thresholds within the same bug regime remains valid.

        \toprule

Very conservative thresholds ($\delta = 0.5$) restrict student exploration excessively, while very permissive thresholds ($\delta = 3.0$) effectively reduce SafeDAgger to DAgger, removing the safety benefit.
At $\delta = 2.0$, the intervention rate decays rapidly (\cref{fig:intervention_rate}), allowing the student to practice autonomously in most states while still receiving teacher guidance on the most challenging transitions.

\subsection{Query Efficiency Analysis}%
\label{sec:ablation_query_efficiency}%

A central claim of SafeDAgger is \emph{query efficiency}: by selectively querying the teacher only on states where the student struggles, SafeDAgger should achieve comparable performance with fewer total teacher queries than vanilla DAgger~\cite{Zhang2016}.
In the original driving domain, SafeDAgger collected 70k targeted examples compared to DAgger's 390k uniformly sampled examples per iteration.

In the dexterous grasping setting, query efficiency is measured by the cumulative number of teacher-executed time steps over the course of distillation.
For DAgger, this equals the total number of time steps (the teacher always provides labels, though the student always acts).
For SafeDAgger, this equals $\beta_{\text{safe}} \times \text{total steps}$, since the teacher only acts in environments where $\|e_t\|_2 > \delta$.

\section{Sim-to-Real Curriculum}%
\label{sec:sim2real_curriculum}%

To close the sim-to-real gap, a second-generation teacher is being trained from scratch with hardware-realistic constraints incorporated into the \ac{ADR} curriculum.

\subsection{Curriculum Design}%
\label{sec:sim2real_design}%

The sim-to-real curriculum adds three categories of hardware constraints that are progressively introduced via \ac{ADR}:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Finger actuator gains:} Per-joint-group stiffness and damping scales, starting at nominal simulation values and widening toward hardware measurements. Initial attempts with hardware-exact gain targets (e.g., MCP pitch stiffness scale down to 0.17) caused policy collapse at ADR levels 16--18 (run~1a); relaxed ranges of $[0.1, 1.0]$ proved stable.
    \item \textbf{Effort and velocity limits:} The thumb rotation velocity limit is ramped from 20\,rad/s (simulation) toward 0.21\,rad/s ($\approx$12\,deg/s, hardware limit). Arm effort limits are reduced toward FR3 factory specs (87\,Nm for joints~1--4, 12\,Nm for joints~5--7).
    \item \textbf{Reward rebalancing:} The object-to-goal weight was doubled ($20 \to 40$) and a success bonus ($w = 10$) was added, since the original reward balance caused the policy to optimize for holding objects rather than delivering them to the goal height (run~1d plateau at in-success-region 0.25--0.35).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Results}%
\label{sec:sim2real_results}%

The sim2real curriculum required 12 iterative runs (1a--1l), each addressing a specific failure mode:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{LSTM checkpoint resume failure (runs~1a--1c):} Attempting to resume from the ADR-19 checkpoint with sim2real constraints caused degradation at ADR levels 16--18, due to LSTM hidden state reset on load (see \cref{sec:ablation_lstm_resume}). This motivated training from scratch.
    \item \textbf{Reward imbalance (runs~1d--1e):} Training from scratch with 1{,}024 environments reached ADR~12 but plateaued, as the policy optimized for grasping over lifting. Doubling the object-to-goal weight and adding a success bonus resolved this (run~1e reached ADR~19 with 2{,}048 envs, though multi-GPU proved $2\times$ slower).
    \item \textbf{Thumb curling into object (runs~1f--1i):} Updated finger gains (stiffness $10 \to 20$, damping $6 \to 2$) caused the thumb to wedge between the object and other fingers. Randomizing the thumb MCP pitch initialization from $-20\degree$ to $0\degree$ (run~1i) significantly reduced this behavior.
    \item \textbf{Contact reward dominance (runs~1j--1l):} Even with the thumb fix, the policy maximized contact reward without lifting. Halving the contact and grasp reward weights and flattening the lift reward sharpness ($5 \to 2$) rebalanced the reward landscape (run~1l).
\end{itemize}

The final sim2real teacher (run~1l/1m, teacher~11) reached ADR level~14 of 50 after 33{,}000 epochs with 1{,}024 environments.
The ADR~14 ceiling reflects the fundamental difficulty of combining hardware-realistic actuator constraints with physics randomization; beyond this level, the reduced finger stiffness and slow thumb velocity make stable grasping infeasible for most object geometries.
Teacher~11 achieves 85.83\% \ac{GSR} and 23.13\% \ac{UER} (12.1\% real \ac{UER} excluding physics instability), which is a lower grasp rate than teacher~10 (96.25\%) but substantially safer (23\% vs.\ 54\% unsafe).
As demonstrated in the distillation results (\cref{sec:distillation_results}), teacher~11 produces superior students despite its lower raw \ac{GSR}, validating the sim2real curriculum approach.

\section{Summary of Findings}%
\label{sec:results_summary}%

The key experimental findings are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{SafeDAgger and DAgger achieve comparable \ac{GSR}.}  With teacher~11 and the one-hot bug fixed (run~9), SafeDAgger (72.7\%) and DAgger (71.7\%) reach similar grasp success rates, achieving $\sim$85\% of the teacher ceiling (85.8\%).
    \item \textbf{SafeDAgger produces safer students.}  The real \ac{UER} (excluding physics instability) is 30.2\% for SafeDAgger vs.\ 32.7\% for DAgger.  During training, the gap is more pronounced: SafeDAgger maintains $\sim$15--20\% real unsafe rate vs.\ $\sim$30--40\% for DAgger (\cref{fig:run9_unsafe_rate}).
    \item \textbf{Teacher quality dominates distillation method.}  Students trained with teacher~11 (sim2real, ADR-14) consistently outperform those trained with teacher~10 (ADR-19), despite teacher~11 having a lower raw \ac{GSR} (85.8\% vs.\ 96.3\%), because its demonstrations are cleaner and contain fewer simulation artifacts.
    \item \textbf{Correct teacher--student object mapping is critical.}  The one-hot index bug fix in run~9 produced +31 percentage points for SafeDAgger and +16.5 for DAgger, demonstrating that teacher observation fidelity is a dominant factor in distillation quality.
    \item \textbf{Safety threshold $\delta = 2.0$ is optimal.}  Among the tested thresholds (0.5, 2.0, 3.0), $\delta = 2.0$ provides the best balance between task performance and safety, allowing the intervention rate to decay rapidly to $\sim$0.1 while maintaining teacher guidance on challenging states.
    \item \textbf{LSTM policies cannot be fine-tuned from checkpoints} for continued ADR curriculum training, due to hidden state reset.  Sim-to-real teachers must be trained from scratch.
    \item \textbf{Physics stability is the primary challenge} for training dexterous grasping policies with custom hand assets.  Actuator parameters (effort limits, damping) must be carefully tuned to prevent contact-avoidance behaviors.
\end{enumerate}
